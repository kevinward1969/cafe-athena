The foundational "Mother Custard" of the pastry kitchen, featuring a rich, velvety texture and a clean vanilla profile.

**Yield:** ~750 g | **Prep:** 15 min | **Inactive:** 4 hr | **Cook:** 10 min | **Total:** ~4.5 hr

## Headnote

Crème Pâtissière (pastry cream) is defined by its use of starch—typically cornstarch—to create a thick, pipeable consistency that is stable at room temperature. A successful pâtissière must be cooked long enough to neutralize specific enzymes while remaining fluid enough to avoid a "rubbery" texture. **Teaching Idea: Starch Gelatinization.** Egg yolks contain an enzyme called alpha-amylase that can break down starch over time, turning a thick cream into a liquid in the refrigerator. Boiling the mixture for a full minute ensures the cornstarch is fully hydrated and the enzymes are thermally destroyed.

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

* **Split and Scrape:** Split the vanilla bean lengthwise and scrape the seeds into the milk/cream mixture.
* **Create the Slurry:** Whisk the cornstarch and a portion of the cold liquid in a small bowl until completely smooth to prevent clumping.
* **Temper the Yolks:** Ensure the egg yolks are at room temperature (70°F/21°C) before beginning the tempering process.
* **Prep the Cooling Station:** Line a small baking sheet with plastic wrap or parchment paper to allow for rapid cooling.

## Ingredients

### The Enriched Base (Optimized for Composites)

* 242 g (1 cup) whole milk
* 242 g (1 cup) heavy cream
* 100 g (½ cup) granulated sugar
* ½ vanilla bean, split and scraped (or 1 tsp vanilla paste)
* 100 g (2 large) eggs, weighed without shells
* 32 g (¼ cup) cornstarch
* 1 g (¼ tsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt
* 15 ml (1 tbsp) Grand Marnier or liquor of choice
* 42 g (3 tbsp) unsalted butter, chilled and cubed

## Method

1. **Infuse and Scald:** In a 3-quart saucepan, combine the milk, heavy cream, sugar, and vanilla bean (seeds and pod). Bring to a simmer over medium-low heat.

1. **Build the Emulsion Base:** While the milk warms, whisk the eggs and cornstarch in a small bowl until perfectly smooth and no white streaks remain.

1. **Temper the Eggs:** Slowly pour roughly 125 ml (½ cup) of the hot milk mixture into the egg mixture while whisking constantly.

   **Why:** This gradually raises the temperature of the eggs, preventing them from scrambling when introduced to the main pot.

1. **The Gelatinization Boil:** Strain the egg mixture through a fine-mesh strainer back into the saucepan. Return to medium heat and whisk constantly, ensuring you reach the corners of the pan.

   **Sensory Cue:** As soon as the mixture thickens and begins to bubble, cook for exactly 60 seconds while whisking vigorously. The texture will shift from matte to glossy.

1. **Finish and Emulsify:** Remove from heat and discard the vanilla pod. Whisk in the Grand Marnier, salt, and chilled butter cubes one at a time until the fat is fully incorporated and the cream is silky.

1. **Rapid Chill:** Pour the hot cream onto the prepared baking sheet and spread thinly. Press a piece of plastic wrap directly onto the surface of the cream.

   **Why:** Surface contact with plastic wrap prevents a "skin" from forming, which would create lumps when the cream is later whisked or piped.

1. **Set:** Cool at room temperature for 30 minutes, then refrigerate for at least 3–4 hours (or overnight) until completely set.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

* **The Standard Base:** For a lighter, traditional pastry cream, replace the heavy cream with an equal amount of whole milk. Increase the cornstarch to 45 g for equivalent stability.
* **Chocolate Variation:** While the cream is still hot (Step 5), whisk in 100 g of chopped 60% dark chocolate. Omit the butter if the chocolate has a high cocoa butter content to maintain the proper set.
* **Storage:** Pastry cream can be stored for up to 3 days. Before using, always "beat" the chilled cream with a whisk to restore its smooth, pipeable consistency.

## Glossary

* **Nappé:** The consistency where a sauce or custard is thick enough to coat the back of a spoon and hold a line when a finger is drawn through it.
* **Syneresis:** The "weeping" of liquid from a gel or cream, often caused by improper starch hydration or enzyme interference.
* **Tempering:** The process of combining two liquids of different temperatures slowly to prevent protein coagulation (scrambling).
