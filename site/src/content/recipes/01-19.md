### **Technique Folio - Global Sourdough Taxonomies**

**Concept Definition:** Not all starters are created equal. The choice of grain (substrate) and hydration (water content) dictates the bacterial dominance (Lactic vs. Acetic), the lifting power, and the final flavor profile.

**The Index: Starter Varietals**

| Style / Name | Origin & Substrate | Strengths (The "Pro") | Weaknesses (The "Con") |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **San Francisco Style** | **Origin:** USA **Grain:** All-Purpose / Bread Flour | **Flavor:** The "Classic" sour profile. High in lactic acid (creamy/yogurt notes). **Utility:** Extremely versatile. Good for pizza, baguettes, and boules. | **Fragility:** White flour lacks nutrients compared to whole grains, making the culture slower to recover from neglect. |
| **The "Desem"** | **Origin:** Belgium **Grain:** Whole Wheat Flour | **Flavor:** Deep, nutty, earthy. Complex "wheaty" aroma. **Power:** Highly active due to the high microbial load on the bran. | **Texture:** The sharp bran flakes cut gluten strands, reducing loaf volume. **Flavor:** Can become bitter or overly acidic if over-fermented. |
| **The Rye (Sauerteig)** | **Origin:** Germany/Scandinavia **Grain:** Whole Rye Flour | **Activity:** "Rocket fuel." Rye's high enzyme content (amylase) creates explosive fermentation. **Keeping Quality:** Adds moisture and shelf-life to baked goods. | **Handling:** Extremely sticky and clay-like. Hard to mix by hand. **Structure:** Rye has almost no gluten; it cannot make a high-rising loaf on its own. |
| **Lievito Madre** | **Origin:** Italy **Grain:** White Flour (Low Hydration) | **Lift:** Incredible strength. Used for massive lifts in heavy doughs like Panettone. **Flavor:** Very mild. Almost no sourness, just pure yeast fragrance. | **Maintenance:** The "Diva" of starters. Requires daily "baths" in sugar water and binding in cloth to control acidity. Very high labor. |
| **Amish Friendship** | **Origin:** USA **Grain:** Flour enriched with Milk/Sugar | **Flavor:** Sweet, cake-like tenderizer. **Speed:** Very fast due to simple sugars. | **Stability:** Unstable. The presence of dairy and sugar invites spoilage organisms (mold) if not used quickly. Often relies on commercial yeast. |
| **Liquid Levain** | **Origin:** France **Hydration:** 100% (Liquid) | **Ease:** Easy to mix and incorporate into dough. **Balance:** Favor lactic acid (mild sourness). | **Peak Window:** Peaks and collapses quickly. |
| **Stiff Levain (Biga)** | **Origin:** France/Italy **Hydration:** 50–60% (Dough) | **Strength:** Holds its peak for hours (slow fermentation). **Flavor:** Favors acetic acid (vinegar-like tang). | **Labor:** Requires kneading rather than just stirring. Harder to dissolve into the final dough. |

**The Science: Why Grain Matters**

* **Buffering Capacity:** Whole Wheat and Rye flours act as a "buffer," meaning they can tolerate higher levels of acid before the pH drops. This allows you to ferment them longer without destroying the dough.
* **Enzymatic Velocity:** Rye ferments fastest; White flour ferments slowest. If you are in a rush, feed your starter Rye. If you want to sleep in, feed it White.
