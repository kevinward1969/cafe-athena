## Headnote

Three foundational herb preparations of classical French cooking — one is always fresh, one always dry, one always removed.

**Yield:** See individual builds | **Prep:** 10–20 min | **Cook:** 0 min (Fines Herbes & Herbes de Provence); 2–4 hr extraction (Bouquet Garni) | **Total:** See individual builds

The moisture state of an herb — fresh, dried, or structural — dictates not only how it is prepared but when it enters the cook and whether it ever reaches the plate. These three blends trace a clean arc: Fines Herbes are cut fresh and folded in at the last possible second, surviving on volatile fragility; Herbes de Provence are built dry, their volatiles concentrated rather than fugitive, deployed at the start of heat; the Bouquet Garni is a structural extract — submerged for hours and pulled before service, leaving no visible trace. Mastering the three together is mastering the logic of when herbs give, when they survive, and when they sacrifice. **Teaching Idea: Volatile Aromatic Degradation.** The flavor and aroma compounds in fresh herbs — linalool in chervil, estragole in tarragon, volatile organosulfur compounds in chives — begin degrading above 60°C/140°F. This is why Fines Herbes are always added off heat: cooking destroys the very compounds that make the blend worth using. Drying arrests this process by reducing water activity, which is why dried blends (Herbes de Provence) tolerate early-cook addition. The Bouquet Garni operates on a third logic — it contributes cineole, thymol, and apigenin into a hot liquid over time, then is removed before those longer-cooking compounds turn bitter.

## Ingredients

## BUILD 1 — FINES HERBES

### The Herb Component

- 4 g (about 2 tbsp loose) fresh flat-leaf parsley, leaves only
- 4 g (about 2 tbsp loose) fresh chives
- 4 g (about 2 tbsp loose) fresh tarragon, leaves only
- 4 g (about 2 tbsp loose) fresh chervil, leaves only

Representative batch: 16 g total (enough for a 4-portion omelette service). Use within 30 minutes of cutting.

---

## BUILD 2 — HERBES DE PROVENCE

### The Blend

- 12 g (4 tbsp / ¼ cup) dried thyme
- 8.5 g (3 tbsp) dried marjoram
- 5 g (2 tbsp) dried savory (summer savory preferred; winter savory is more resinous — reduce quantity by 20% if substituting)
- 4 g (1½ tbsp) dried rosemary, coarsely crumbled (not powdered)
- 2.5 g (1 tbsp) dried oregano
- 1.5 g (1½ tsp) dried fennel seed, lightly cracked in a mortar

Total: ~34 g | Yield: ~34 g. Shelf life: 6 months in airtight jar, away from heat and light.

---

## BUILD 3 — BOUQUET GARNI

### Per Bundle

- 1 dried bay leaf (Laurus nobilis — see Chef's Notes on Turkish vs. California bay)
- 3–4 sprigs fresh thyme
- 4–5 fresh flat-leaf parsley stems (stems only; reserve leaves for Fines Herbes or finishing)
- kitchen twine, approximately 30 cm (12 in)

Yield: 1 bundle per recipe. Remove before serving; leaves no residue.

---

## Mise en Place

- Wash all fresh herbs under cold water. Dry completely on paper towels or in a salad spinner — surface moisture causes browning and accelerates volatile loss.
- Strip parsley and tarragon leaves from stems; reserve parsley stems for the Bouquet Garni build.
- Set out kitchen twine, scissors, and a mortar if making Herbes de Provence.
- Set out an airtight 4 oz / 120 ml mason jar and a label if making Herbes de Provence for storage.
- Have all dried herb components measured and ready before combining.

---

## Method

### Build 1: Fines Herbes — Cutting and Combining

Chiffonade parsley leaves: stack, roll tightly, and cut across the roll in 1–2 mm ribbons. Repeat with tarragon. Cut chives with scissors directly into the bowl — scissors produce a clean cross-section and avoid the bruising a knife causes on a cylindrical stem. Add chervil last: it is the most fragile herb in the blend. Fold it in with a single gentle toss rather than stirring; its feathery leaves bruise and darken under pressure.

Combine all four with a light hand. The finished blend should be vivid green with no brown edges. If any herb has begun to oxidize at the cut ends, it is past its window — cut fresh.

Add at the moment of plating or immediately after pulling a pan sauce off heat. The target is below 60°C/140°F at the moment of contact. Never add mid-cook. The volatile compounds responsible for the blend's brightness — linalool in chervil, estragole in tarragon, volatile organosulfur compounds in chives — degrade rapidly above that threshold and do not recover.

### Build 2: Herbes de Provence — Blending and Storing

Crack fennel seed once or twice in a mortar — the goal is to open the seed and release anethole without reducing to powder. Tap lightly: you want cracked, not crushed. If fennel is dominant on the nose after cracking, it has been worked too fine; the whole blend will read anise-forward in use.

Combine all dried ingredients in a bowl and toss until evenly distributed. No toasting — additional heat drives off the remaining volatiles in already-dried herbs. The blend should smell thyme-forward, with resinous rosemary as a secondary note and a lift of fennel anise in the background. If rosemary dominates, the crumble was too coarse and it is releasing its resin disproportionately; re-crumble finer and redistribute.

Funnel into the airtight jar. Label with the date. In use: add at the beginning of cooking. Rub onto meat with olive oil and Diamond Crystal kosher salt before roasting, add to sautéed aromatics before liquid goes in, or toss with vegetables before the sheet pan.

### Build 3: Bouquet Garni — Binding and Extracting

Lay the bay leaf flat. Stack the thyme sprigs and parsley stems on top, keeping the bundle compact and uniform in diameter — a loose bundle will lose components during a long braise. Bind with twine at the center and again near one end. Leave a 20–25 cm tail to tie to the pot handle; this tail is your retrieval mechanism and eliminates any chance of losing the bundle in a full stock pot.

Add the bundle as the liquid goes in — not during the browning phase, where dry herbs will scorch. Submerge completely in stocks; nestle alongside protein in braises.

Remove 15–20 minutes before final reduction or finishing. Beyond 2–3 hours in a braise or 4 hours in a stock, the bundle will extract bitter tannins from stems. When pulled, the bundle should smell muted and slightly vegetal — it has given what it has to give. If it still smells bright and herbal at that point, it went in too late.

**Cheesecloth sachet variation:** When a recipe requires loose additions (whole peppercorns, dried herbs without structure, spice fragments), wrap in a double layer of cheesecloth and tie securely. The sachet method keeps the liquid clear and makes retrieval certain.

## Variations

**Escoffier Grand Bouquet:** Fold a leek green around the thyme-parsley bundle before tying — the leek acts as a natural wrapper that holds the bundle together and contributes a mild allium note without the raw sulfur edge of a cut leek. Add a piece of celery stalk and 8–10 black peppercorns wrapped in cheesecloth alongside. This is the restaurant-standard build for brown stocks and long braises.

**Restaurant Fines Herbes (Chervil-Weighted Ratio):** Shift to approximately 40% chervil, 20% each of parsley, chives, and tarragon. This compensates for chervil's rapid volatile fade during multi-seat service — more chervil in the initial cut means more anise-forward character survives to the plate by the time the last covers go out. Equal parts is correct for home service; lean chervil-forward for staged restaurant service.

## Chef's Notes

**Lavender in Herbes de Provence:** Absent from the oldest Provençal formulas. Added to commercial export blends from the mid-20th century onward, primarily for the US and UK markets. At roughly 3–5% by weight (~½ g in a 34 g batch, about ½ tsp), it contributes a floral note that integrates in fatty, long-cooked applications — lamb braises, daubes. In lighter dishes (grilled fish, eggs, delicate vegetables), it reads as soap. Keep under 3% by weight if you choose to add it. Above that threshold it becomes the dominant note.

**Turkish vs. California bay:** Turkish bay (Laurus nobilis) is what classical French cooking requires and what the Bouquet Garni builds assume. California bay (Umbellularia californica) is 2–3× more potent per leaf — using one California leaf where a Turkish leaf is called for produces an aggressively medicinal result. Supermarket bulk bay is almost certainly Turkish. Home-grown West Coast bay is almost certainly California. When in doubt, halve the quantity.

**Extended Bouquet Garni:** Classical sources list basil, burnet, chervil, rosemary, peppercorns, savory, and tarragon as optional additions. Rosemary is strongly resinous and non-receding — use only when you want a pronounced herbal note in the finished dish. Basil blackens and turns bitter in long cooks; add it only in the last 30 minutes, if at all.

**Make-ahead:** Fines Herbes cannot be made ahead — 30 minutes is the outer limit from cut to plate. Herbes de Provence can be batched months in advance. Bouquet Garni bundles can be assembled up to 24 hours ahead and refrigerated loosely wrapped; do not soak them in advance or the herbs will begin to macerate.

## Glossary

- Fines Herbes: The classical French four-herb blend of parsley, chives, tarragon, and chervil, always used fresh and always added off heat.
- Herbes de Provence: A dry herb blend from the Provence region of France, built around thyme, marjoram, savory, rosemary, and oregano; deployed at the start of cooking.
- Bouquet Garni: A bundle of fresh herbs (typically bay, thyme, and parsley stems) tied and submerged in stocks or braises to extract aromatic compounds over a long cook, then removed before service.
- Chiffonade: A cutting technique in which leafy herbs or greens are stacked, rolled tightly, and sliced crosswise into thin ribbons.
- Volatile Aromatic Compounds: Flavor and aroma molecules that evaporate readily at cooking temperatures. In fresh herbs, these include linalool (chervil), estragole (tarragon), and organosulfur compounds (chives); heat above 60°C/140°F causes rapid degradation.
- Linalool: A terpene alcohol responsible for the light floral-herbal character of chervil and some thyme varieties. Highly heat-sensitive.
- Estragole: The primary volatile compound in tarragon, responsible for its anise-adjacent aroma. Degrades quickly above 60°C/140°F; also present in basil.
- Anethole: The primary flavor compound in fennel seed and star anise, responsible for their characteristic anise character. Fat-soluble and relatively stable compared to green-herb volatiles.
- Cineole: Also called eucalyptol; the dominant aromatic compound in bay leaf, contributing the deep camphor-adjacent "stock flavor" characteristic of classical French sauces.
- Thymol: The primary phenolic compound in thyme, responsible for its warm, slightly antiseptic herbal note. More heat-stable than most terpene volatiles, which is why thyme can be cooked long without losing all character.
- Apigenin: A flavonoid found in parsley stems that acts as a carrier compound in stocks and braises, helping other aromatic molecules stay dissolved and distributed through the liquid.
- Laurus nobilis: The botanical name for true (Turkish/Mediterranean) bay laurel — the bay leaf used in classical French cooking and all recipes in this book.
- Umbellularia californica: California bay laurel; related to but distinct from Laurus nobilis, with essential oil 2–3× more concentrated. Not a direct substitute at the same quantity.
- Water Activity: A measure of how much free (unbound) water is available in a food; reducing water activity through drying preserves dried herbs by limiting the microbial and enzymatic reactions that degrade volatiles.
