# Café Athena - Pâte Brisée (Classic Shortcrust)

The universal savory crust. Tender, buttery, and sturdy enough to hold heavy quiche batters or meat fillings.

**Yield:** 2 Disks (enough for one 9-inch double crust or two tarts) | **Prep:** 15 min | **Inactive:** 1 hr | **Cook:** N/A

---

## Headnote

This dough relies on the "Sablage" (sanding) method. By coating the flour particles in fat *before* adding water, we waterproof the proteins, inhibiting gluten formation.

**Teaching Idea: Fraisage (The Smear).** Unlike bread dough, which we knead to build strength, we finish pastry dough by smearing it across the table with the heel of the hand. This creates long, thin streaks of butter (flakiness) without activating the gluten (toughness).

---

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

- **Chill the blade:** If using a food processor, place the metal blade in the freezer for 10 minutes.
- **Dice the butter:** Cut butter into 1cm (½ inch) cubes and return to the refrigerator until the moment of use.
- **Prepare the ice water:** Fill a cup with water and ice cubes; measure the water from this freezing mixture.
- **Clear the bench:** Ensure you have a cool, clean surface (marble or stainless steel is ideal) for the fraisage.

---

## Ingredients

### The Dry Mix

- 340 g (2¾ cups) All-Purpose Flour
- 6 g (1½ tsp) Diamond Crystal Kosher Salt
- 10 g (2 tsp) Granulated Sugar (optional, for browning)

### The Fat & Liquid

- 225 g (1 cup / 2 sticks) Unsalted Butter, cold and cubed
- 80–120 g (⅓ – ½ cup) Ice Water

---

## Method

**Phase 1: Sablage (The Sanding)** Place the flour, salt, and sugar in the bowl of a food processor. Pulse twice to combine. Scatter the cold butter cubes over the flour. Pulse 8–10 times.

> **Sensory Cue:** Stop when the mixture looks like coarse yellow cornmeal, with some butter pieces the size of peas still visible. **Why:** If you process until smooth, the crust will be crumbly (mealy) rather than flaky. The pea-sized butter bits create steam pockets.

**Phase 2: Hydration** Transfer the mixture to a large wide bowl. Drizzle 80g (⅓ cup) of the ice water over the surface. Toss gently with a fork or your spread fingers—do not squeeze. The goal is to distribute moisture evenly. Squeeze a small handful of dough. If it holds together, stop. If it crumbles apart, add more water, 1 tablespoon at a time, tossing between additions.

**Phase 3: Fraisage (The Structure)** Dump the shaggy crumbs onto your work surface. It will look dry and unmanageable—this is correct. Using the heel of your hand, push small portions of the dough away from you, smearing it against the table.

> **Why:** This final mechanical action crushes the butter chunks into flat layers and hydrates the flour without the heat or friction of kneading.

Gather the smeared dough pieces back together. They should now bond into a cohesive mass.

**Phase 4: Scaling and Resting** Divide the dough into two equal portions. Shape each into a flat disk (not a ball). Wrap tightly in plastic wrap. Refrigerate for at least **1 hour** (up to 2 days) before rolling.

> **Why:** The flour needs time to absorb the water fully, and the butter must firm up again to ensure flaky layers during baking.

---

## Chef's Notes / Variations

- **Pâte Sucrée (Sweet Variation):** Add 50g (¼ cup) sugar to the dry mix and replace 2 tablespoons of water with one egg yolk. This creates a richer, cookie-like dough for fruit tarts.
- **The Temperature Rule:** If at any point the butter feels greasy or the dough becomes sticky, stop immediately. Put everything (dough, rolling pin, hands) in the fridge for 10 minutes.
- **Blind Baking:** Because this dough has a high fat content, it will slump if baked empty. Always line with parchment and fill with pie weights (or dried beans) when pre-baking.

---

## Glossary

- **Sablage:** The "sanding" method of mixing fat into flour until it resembles sand, coating the proteins to shorten the gluten strands.
- **Fraisage:** A French technique of smearing dough with the heel of the hand to blend fat and flour without overworking the gluten.
- **Blind Bake:** Baking a pie crust without the filling (using weights) to ensure the bottom is crisp.
