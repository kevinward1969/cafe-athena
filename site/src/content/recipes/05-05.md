**Café Athena - Classic Hot-Smoked Salmon**

Glossy, maple-lacquered salmon with a firm, flaky bite and a deep amber pellicle that holds the smoke.

**Yield:** 4 portions (6–8 oz each) | **Prep:** 20 min | **Inactive:** 8–12 hr | **Cook:** 1–1.5 hr | **Total:** ~10–14 hr

## Headnote

A dry-cured, low-and-slow hot smoke finished with a maple–brown sugar glaze applied directly on the smoker. **Teaching Idea: The Pellicle.** Before any smoke touches the fish, the surface needs to air-dry into a tacky, slightly matte film. Smoke compounds bond to that tacky protein layer, not to wet flesh — skip it, and you get smoke smell without smoke flavor.

## Ingredients

### The Dry Cure
* 100 g (½ cup) Diamond Crystal kosher salt
* 100 g (½ cup) light brown sugar

### The Fish
* 4 salmon portions, 6–8 oz each, skin-on, pin bones removed

### The Baste
* 60 ml (¼ cup) pure maple syrup
* 30 g (2 tbsp) light brown sugar

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

* Combine salt and sugar for the dry cure
* Pat salmon portions dry
* Line a sheet tray with parchment for curing
* Set a wire rack over a second sheet tray for the pellicle rest
* Whisk maple syrup and brown sugar together for the baste; set aside at room temperature
* Soak wood chunks (offset) or load the pellet hopper with alder or apple

## Method

1. **Cure:** Pack each salmon portion evenly on all sides with the dry cure mixture.

1. Place skin-side down on the parchment-lined sheet tray, uncovered, and refrigerate **6–8 hours**.

   **Why:** Osmosis draws surface moisture out and begins firming the flesh — this is what gives hot-smoked salmon its characteristic denser bite versus a raw filet.

1. **Rinse:** Rinse off all visible cure under cold water.

1. Pat the portions completely dry.

1. **Pellicle:** Transfer to the wire rack set over a sheet tray, uncovered, and refrigerate **4–6 hours** until the surface looks slightly matte and feels tacky, not wet.

1. **Smoke:** Bring the smoker to a steady 175°F/80°C. On an offset, maintain airflow for thin blue smoke, not thick white — thick smoke means incomplete combustion and bitterness. On a pellet grill, set to the 175°F/80°C smoke setting; expect a milder smoke character than the offset at the same temperature. Place portions skin-side down and smoke until internal temperature reaches **125°F/52°C**, about 45–60 minutes depending on thickness.

1. **Glaze:** Brush the maple baste generously over each portion. Continue smoking until internal temperature reaches **140–145°F/60–63°C**, another 10–15 minutes.

   **Sensory Cue:** Flesh should flake easily at the thickest point, and the glaze should look set and glossy, not runny.

1. **Rest:** Rest 5 minutes before serving.

## Variations

* **Wet Brine Variation:** Dissolve 1 cup Diamond Crystal kosher salt and 1 cup brown sugar per gallon of cold water. Submerge portions fully and refrigerate 4–6 hours (thinner portions) up to 8 hours (thicker cuts). Rinse, pat dry, and proceed from the Rinse and Pellicle step onward. Produces a slightly softer, more evenly seasoned result than the dry cure.

## Chef's Notes

* **Flavor Kick:** For a brighter, sharper version of the glaze, whisk in 1 tsp Dijon mustard, fresh-cracked black pepper, and the zest of half a lemon along with the maple and brown sugar.
* **Offset vs. Pellet:** The offset gives a more pronounced smoke ring and bark-like glaze edge due to combustion variability; the pellet grill gives a more consistent, repeatable result with a softer smoke presence. Both hit the same internal temperature targets — adjust by feel, not just by clock.
* **Doneness:** Pull at 140°F/60°C if serving immediately (carryover gets you to 145°F/63°C); pull at 145°F/63°C directly if holding before serving.

## Glossary

* Pellicle: The tacky, dried protein film that forms on the surface of cured fish before smoking, which smoke compounds adhere to.
* Hot Smoke: A smoking method that cooks food fully while imparting smoke flavor, typically 140–175°F/60–80°C chamber temperature.
* Dry Cure: A curing method using a salt/sugar mixture applied directly to the food's surface, drawing out moisture via osmosis.
