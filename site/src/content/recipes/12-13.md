The definitive guide to whipped cream, from simple vanilla-flecked clouds to structurally reinforced aerated fats.

**Yield:** ~900 ml | **Prep:** 10 min | **Cook:** 0 min | **Total:** 10 min

## Headnote

Crème Chantilly is heavy cream that has been aerated and sweetened. Its success depends entirely on "Cold Chain Management"—keeping the fat in a solid state so it can form a rigid lattice around air bubbles. **Teaching Idea: The Fat Lattice.** Whipping cream causes the protective membranes around fat globules to partially break, allowing the fat to "stick" together into a structural network. If the cream is warm, the fat is liquid and cannot form this lattice, resulting in a flat, greasy soup rather than a foam.

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

* **Deep Chill:** Place the mixing bowl and whisk attachment in the freezer for 10 minutes prior to use.
* **Check the Percentage:** Ensure your heavy cream is between 36% and 40% milkfat; "light" creams lack the fat density to hold air.
* **Sift the Sugar:** Sift powdered sugar to remove any cornstarch lumps that may interfere with the texture.
* **Hydrate Stabilizers:** If using gelatin, bloom the sheet in ice water for at least 5 minutes.

## Ingredients

### The Standard Chantilly

* 480 g (2 cups) heavy cream, minimum 36% fat, very cold
* 50 g (¼ cup) powdered sugar
* 5 ml (1 tsp) vanilla extract or vanilla bean paste

### Structural Variations (Optional)

* **Xanthan Stabilizer:** 0.5 g (⅛ tsp) xanthan gum (mixed with the sugar).
* **Gelatin Reinforcement:** 2 g (1 sheet) Gold-strength gelatin, melted into 15 ml of warm cream.
* **Mascarpone Enrichment:** 60 g (¼ cup) cold mascarpone.

## Method

1. **The Cold Start:** Pour the cold heavy cream, sugar, and vanilla into the chilled mixing bowl. 

1. **Slow Aeration:** Start the mixer on medium-low speed. 

   **Why:** A slower start builds a smaller, more uniform bubble structure. This "fine crumb" makes the final whipped cream significantly more stable than cream whipped quickly on high speed.

1. **Incorporate Stabilizers:** If using the Gelatin or Xanthan variations, add them once the cream begins to show "soft trails" (faint lines from the whisk that quickly disappear).

1. **Whisk to Peak:** Increase the speed to medium-high. 

   **Sensory Cue:** The cream will shift from a liquid to a matte, pillowy solid. The whisk will leave sharp, distinct tracks.

1. **Test the Set:** Stop the mixer when you reach **Medium-Stiff Peaks** (the peak holds its shape but the very tip softly curls) if folding into a custard, or **Stiff Peaks** (the peak stands perfectly straight) for piping.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

* **Over-whipping Fix:** If the cream looks grainy or "broken" (approaching butter), add 1–2 tablespoons of fresh, liquid cold cream and fold it in gently by hand with a spatula to smooth the emulsion.
* **The "Open Life":** Stabilized Chantilly (using xanthan or gelatin) can be piped onto a cake and will hold its sharp edges for 24–48 hours without weeping.

## Glossary

* **Syneresis:** The process where water is squeezed out of a foam or gel, commonly known as "weeping."
* **Milkfat Percentage:** The amount of fat in the cream; 36% is the professional standard for stable piping.
* **Overrun:** The volume of air incorporated into the cream; professional Chantilly usually has an overrun of nearly 100% (doubling in volume).
