Low-and-slow braised collard greens cooked in a smoked ham hock broth until silky, with a sharp apple cider vinegar finish and pot likker reserved for serving.

**Yield:** 6 servings | **Prep:** 20 min | **Cook:** 2 hr 15 min | **Total:** 2 hr 35 min

---

## Headnote

Southern braised collard greens are one of the most misunderstood preparations in American cooking — dismissed as simple, they are in fact a precise extraction exercise. The real objective is the pot likker: the braising liquid that accumulates over two-plus hours of simmering ham hock, aromatic vegetables, and tough greens. As the smoked hock cooks, collagen in the bone and connective tissue converts to gelatin, enriching the liquid with body and a lingering savory depth that no amount of added stock can replicate. The greens themselves absorb this liquid as they braise, which is why collards cooked in plain water or store-bought broth never taste the same. A small measure of sugar near the end of cooking is not sweetness for its own sake — it is a tannin cut, tempering the natural bitterness of raw collards that survives even a two-hour braise. The apple cider vinegar finish is added off-heat and is non-negotiable: it lifts the entire pot, balancing richness and providing brightness in the same way a squeeze of lemon finishes a cream sauce.

**Teaching Idea: Collagen-to-Gelatin Conversion.** Collagen is the primary structural protein in connective tissue and bones. When held at temperatures between 160°F/71°C and 180°F/82°C over an extended period — typically 1½ to 3 hours depending on the cut — collagen denatures and converts to gelatin, a water-soluble protein that dissolves into the braising liquid. This is what gives pot likker its silky, almost coating quality. A liquid that begins as thin stock finishes as something closer to a light jus. This is the same mechanism behind the body in a well-made bone broth, a French pot-au-feu, or a Neapolitan Sunday ragù.

## Ingredients

### The Aromatics

- 170 g (6 oz) thick-cut bacon, cut into 1.5 cm (½ in) lardons
- 1 medium yellow onion, finely diced (approximately 200 g)
- 5 garlic cloves, thinly sliced
- 3 g (½ tsp) red pepper flakes
- 1 g (¼ tsp) freshly ground black pepper

### The Braise

- 1 smoked ham hock (approximately 400–500 g / 14–18 oz)
- 960 ml (4 cups) low-sodium chicken stock
- 480 ml (2 cups) water
- 10 g (2 tsp) granulated sugar
- 6 g (1 tsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt, plus more to taste

### The Greens

- 900 g (2 lb) fresh collard greens (approximately 2 large bunches)
- Diamond Crystal kosher salt, for seasoning

### The Finish

- 30 ml (2 tbsp) apple cider vinegar
- Flaky sea salt, for finishing

### For Serving

- Hot sauce (Crystal or Tabasco), to taste

---

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

- **Cut the bacon** into 1.5 cm (½ in) lardons and set aside.
- **Dice the onion** finely and set aside.
- **Slice the garlic** thinly.
- **Strip and chop the collard greens:** Hold each leaf stem-side up. Fold the leaf in half along the central rib, then tear or cut the leaf away from the rib. Discard the ribs. Stack the stripped leaves in batches and cut crosswise into roughly 5 cm (2 in) ribbons. Wash thoroughly in two changes of cold water, agitating well. Drain but do not dry — some residual moisture is fine.
- **Measure and combine** the chicken stock and water in a large vessel, ready to add.
- **Measure** the sugar and salt into a small prep bowl.
- **Have a ladle and a deep, heavy pot** (at least 6-quart / 5.7 L capacity) ready.

---

## Method

**Phase 1: Render the Bacon.** Place the heavy pot over medium heat. Add the bacon lardons in a single layer. Cook, stirring occasionally, for 8–10 minutes until the fat is fully rendered and the bacon pieces are golden and crisp.

**Sensory Cue:** The bacon should be deeply golden — not just pale — and the fat in the pan should be clear, not cloudy. The sizzling will settle considerably as the water in the bacon cooks off; this is when the Maillard reaction is in full effect on the meat proteins. Remove the bacon with a slotted spoon and set aside, leaving all the rendered fat in the pot.

**Phase 2: Build the Aromatic Base.** Add the diced onion to the rendered fat in the pot. Increase heat to medium-high. Cook, stirring occasionally, for 6–8 minutes until the onion is soft and translucent at the edges, with slight golden color developing.

**Sensory Cue:** The onion should collapse and turn glossy. You are looking for the beginning of caramelization at the edges — not fully browned, but past the raw-soft stage. Add the sliced garlic and red pepper flakes. Cook for 1–2 minutes more, stirring constantly.

**Sensory Cue:** The garlic should turn lightly golden at the edges and become fragrant — approximately 60 seconds. Do not let the garlic brown fully; it will become bitter under the long braise.

**Phase 3: Add the Hock and Liquid.** Nestle the smoked ham hock into the pot. Pour in the chicken stock and water. Add the black pepper. Bring to a full boil over high heat, then reduce to a steady, low simmer — small bubbles breaking the surface regularly, not a rolling boil.

**Sensory Cue:** The liquid should shimmer and just barely move. A full rolling boil will toughen the collards and emulsify fat in the liquid rather than letting it clarify. Set the lid ajar (half-covering the pot) to allow some evaporation and concentration.

**Phase 4: Braise the Greens.** Working in batches if necessary, add the prepared collard ribbons to the pot, pushing them down as they wilt to make room for the next addition. The pot will seem impossibly full at first — this is correct. Cover and cook for 5 minutes, then remove the lid and stir to redistribute.

Once all the greens are submerged in the liquid, replace the lid ajar. Cook on low heat for 1 hour 30 minutes to 1 hour 45 minutes, stirring every 20–25 minutes.

**Sensory Cue:** At 90 minutes, the greens should be very dark — an olive-khaki green, fully collapsed, and tender when pressed between your fingers. They should offer no resistance, but should not be completely dissolving. The braising liquid should have reduced by roughly one-third and turned from pale stock to a noticeably deeper, slightly opaque pot likker. Taste it: it should be savory, smoky, and rounded.

**Why:** The long braise is non-negotiable for collards specifically. Unlike spinach or chard, collards are dense with cellulose and require sustained heat to break down. Greens cooked for less than 90 minutes will have a slightly fibrous chew and a more aggressive bitterness.

**Phase 5: Season and Finish.** Add the sugar and salt to the pot. Stir well. Taste the braising liquid and adjust salt as needed. Simmer uncovered for 10 minutes more to integrate the seasoning and allow any remaining liquid to concentrate.

Remove the ham hock. Using two forks, pull any meat from the hock and return it to the pot; discard the bone and skin.

Remove the pot from the heat. Add the apple cider vinegar. Stir, then taste. The vinegar should lift the pot — the underlying sweetness and richness should come into focus rather than recede. If the pot tastes flat, add an additional 5 ml (1 tsp) of vinegar.

**Why:** Adding acid off-heat prevents the volatile aromatic compounds in the vinegar from cooking off. The acidity performs two functions simultaneously: it balances the residual fat from the bacon and hock, and it suppresses the perception of bitterness from the greens.

**Phase 6: Serve.** Ladle the collards into bowls with a generous amount of pot likker. Return the reserved crispy bacon lardons to each bowl or scatter over the top. Finish with a pinch of flaky sea salt and hot sauce alongside.

## Variations

### Mustard Greens Version

Replace the collard greens with an equal weight of fresh mustard greens, prepared the same way. Reduce the braise time in Phase 4 to 45–55 minutes — mustard greens are thinner-leaved and cook substantially faster than collards. The flavor profile shifts: mustard greens have a sharp, peppery bite that collards lack, which means you may want to reduce or omit the red pepper flakes and reduce the sugar to 5 g (1 tsp). The pot likker will be lighter in body and more assertively pungent. This is a genuinely different dish — serve it as the sharper, more aggressive counterpart to the collard version.

### Smoked Turkey Version (Dairy-Free)

Replace the bacon and ham hock with 1 smoked turkey leg (approximately 600–700 g / 21–25 oz). Omit the rendered-fat stage entirely — add 15 ml (1 tbsp) of neutral oil to the pot to sauté the aromatics before adding the turkey leg and liquid. The resulting pot likker will be lighter in fat but equally collagen-rich, with a cleaner smoke note and a less porcine character. Dietary qualification: Gluten-Free, Dairy-Free.

## Chef's Notes

- **Stock vs. water ratio:** The 4:2 stock-to-water ratio is deliberate. All-stock braising liquid can turn cloying after two hours; the water provides dilution that allows the hock's own flavor to dominate without competing with the stock's seasoning. If using homemade, well-reduced stock, increase the water proportion.
- **The pot likker is the dish:** Do not discard the braising liquid. It should be ladled generously into the bowl alongside the greens. In traditional Southern cooking, the pot likker is served separately for dipping cornbread — a practice worth preserving. A skillet cornbread is the traditional vessel.
- **Make-ahead:** This dish improves significantly on day two. Cool completely, refrigerate in the braising liquid overnight, and reheat gently over low heat. The fat will solidify on the surface and can be skimmed for a cleaner pot likker, or stirred back in for a richer one. Season again after reheating.
- **Sourcing the hock:** Smoked ham hocks are available at most grocery stores year-round in the pork section. Look for one with good color — deep rose to burgundy on the cut face — and a genuine smoke aroma. Avoid any labeled simply "cured" without smoke, as they will not produce the same pot likker character.
- **Scaling:** Ingredients scale linearly. The braise time does not — a doubled batch in the same pot may require 15–20 additional minutes at Phase 4 because the volume-to-surface-area ratio increases. Monitor by texture, not the clock.
- **Acid adjustment:** Different brands of apple cider vinegar vary in acidity. Start with 30 ml (2 tbsp) and taste before adding more. The goal is a perceptible lift, not sourness.

## Glossary

- Pot Likker: The braising liquid produced when greens, smoked pork, and aromatics are cooked together over low heat for an extended period. The liquid concentrates flavor from the pork, gelatin from the collagen, and leached compounds from the greens, producing a savory, silky broth served alongside the greens or used for dunking cornbread.
- Collagen: The primary structural protein found in connective tissue, skin, and bones. When held at sustained low heat (160°F–180°F / 71°C–82°C), collagen denatures and converts to gelatin, adding body and richness to braising liquids.
- Gelatin: A water-soluble protein produced by the hydrolysis of collagen during low-and-slow cooking. Gelatin dissolved in liquid produces a silky mouthfeel and a coating quality that water or stock alone cannot replicate.
- Lardons: Small strips or cubes of bacon or salt pork used for rendering fat and adding flavor and texture to a dish. The rendered fat (drippings) becomes the cooking medium for subsequent aromatics.
- Rendered Fat: Fat that has been melted out of a protein (bacon, duck, ham) by gentle heat, leaving behind crisped solids and a pure cooking fat in the pan.
- Tannin: Naturally occurring polyphenol compounds found in leafy greens (particularly collards, kale, and mustard greens) that produce a dry, bitter sensation on the palate. Sugar added late in the braise suppresses the perception of tannin without making the dish sweet.
- Maillard Reaction: The chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that produces brown color and complex roasted flavor when food is exposed to sufficient dry heat. Drives the browning of bacon in Phase 1.
- Fond: The caramelized residue left on the bottom of a pan after rendering or sautéing. In this recipe, the fond from the bacon and onion contributes base-level depth to the braising liquid as it dissolves.
