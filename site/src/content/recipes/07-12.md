Hickory-kissed commercial bacon transformed into snackable, garnish-ready strips through deep flavor brining and dehydrator precision.

**Yield:** ~20–24 strips | **Prep:** 20 min | **Inactive:** 24 hr brine + 6–10 hr dehydrate | **Total:** ~31–35 hr

## Headnote

Commercial hickory-smoked bacon carries a head start — it's already cured, already kissed with smoke, and sliced to a consistent thickness. Our job is to hijack that foundation and drive new flavor deep into the fat and muscle before the dehydrator does its work. A 24-hour flavor brine opens the meat's protein structure and seasons from within; the dry rub then builds a concentrated surface crust as moisture evacuates during dehydration. **Teaching Idea: The Two-Stage Flavor Stack.** Wet brining deposits water-soluble flavor compounds (salts, sugars, aromatics) into the interior of the meat through osmotic diffusion. The subsequent dry rub targets the surface, where heat and moisture loss will concentrate those flavors into a caramelized, aromatic crust — a result neither method achieves alone.

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

- Pull bacon from packaging and separate all strips; pat dry with paper towels
- Prepare brine ingredients and whisk to combine in a non-reactive container (glass or food-safe plastic)
- Submerge all strips fully in brine; weigh down with a plate or press-sealed bag if needed
- Cover and refrigerate for 24 hours
- After brining, remove strips and rinse briefly under cold water; pat completely dry
- Prepare your chosen dry rub blend in a shallow bowl
- Set dehydrator to 160°F/71°C and allow to preheat
- Line dehydrator trays — do not overlap strips

## Ingredients

### The Base Brine (Shared by All Four Profiles)

*Scale to fully submerge your bacon — this ratio is per 1 lb/450 g of strips*

- 240 ml (1 cup) regular soy sauce
- 240 ml (1 cup) water
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) rice vinegar
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) Worcestershire sauce
- 4 cloves garlic, smashed
- 5 g (1 tsp) cracked black pepper
- 5 ml (1 tsp) toasted sesame oil *(omit for Profiles 1 & 2)*

### Profile 1 — Classic Salt & Smoke (Dry Rub)

- 6 g (2 tsp) smoked paprika
- 3 g (1 tsp) garlic powder
- 3 g (1 tsp) onion powder
- 3 g (1 tsp) coarse black pepper
- 1 g (½ tsp) cayenne pepper *(optional, for background heat)*

### Profile 2 — Sweet & Salty (Dry Rub)

- 12 g (1 tbsp) dark brown sugar, packed
- 3 g (1 tsp) smoked paprika
- 3 g (1 tsp) dry mustard powder
- 2 g (½ tsp) garlic powder
- 2 g (½ tsp) coarse black pepper

### Profile 3 — Sweet & Spicy (Dry Rub)

- 12 g (1 tbsp) dark brown sugar, packed
- 6 g (2 tsp) gochugaru *(or substitute 3 g/1 tsp cayenne for sharper heat)*
- 3 g (1 tsp) smoked paprika
- 2 g (½ tsp) coarse black pepper
- 2 g (½ tsp) garlic powder

### Profile 4 — Asian-Leaning (Dry Rub)

- 8 g (1 tbsp) light brown sugar
- 4 g (1 tsp) Chinese five spice
- 2 g (½ tsp) ground white pepper
- 2 g (½ tsp) ground ginger
- 4 g (1 tsp) toasted sesame seeds

## Method

### Phase 1 – The Brine

Combine all brine ingredients in a non-reactive container and whisk until the soy and water are fully integrated. Submerge the bacon strips completely — use a small plate or a zip-lock bag with air pressed out to keep strips below the surface. Refrigerate for 24 hours.

**Sensory Cue:** The brine will take on a slightly darker, more opaque appearance as proteins leach from the bacon. This is normal and desirable.

### Phase 2 – Rinse & Dry

Remove strips from the brine and rinse briefly under cold running water to remove surface salinity. Pat every strip completely dry with paper towels — press firmly. This step is non-negotiable.

**Why:** Residual surface moisture creates steam in the dehydrator rather than dry heat, dramatically slowing fat rendering and producing a greasy rather than crisp strip.

### Phase 3 – Apply the Dry Rub

Working one strip at a time, press your chosen dry rub firmly onto both sides of each strip. You want visible, even coverage — not a light dusting. Place rubbed strips directly onto dehydrator trays with no overlap.

**Sensory Cue:** Strips should look matte and well-coated, not wet or shiny. If the rub is sliding off, the strip isn't dry enough — return to paper towels.

### Phase 4 – Dehydrate

Run the dehydrator at 160°F/71°C. Begin checking at the 6-hour mark.

**Doneness Indicators — check all three:**

- **Visual:** Strip is visibly reduced in width; edges show slight curl; surface is dry and matte with no visible fat pooling
- **Tactile:** Strip is firm and rigid when lifted from one end; it may flex slightly but should not droop or feel pliable
- **Sound:** A dry strip will produce a faint, papery crinkle when bent — not a wet, soft give

Thick-cut strips will typically require 7–10 hours depending on ambient humidity and dehydrator air circulation. Do not rush this.

### Phase 5 – Rest & Set

Remove finished strips to a wire rack and allow to cool completely at room temperature — minimum 20 minutes. Strips will firm further as residual fat solidifies.

**Why:** Hot strips fresh from the dehydrator will feel softer than their final state. Evaluating doneness while still hot leads to over-dehydrating.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

- **On Soy Sauce and Salt:** Regular soy sauce is used here intentionally — it provides depth and umami without the additional salt adjustments required by reduced-sodium products. Soy sauce brands vary significantly in sodium content; taste your brine before submerging the bacon. It should be assertively seasoned but not punishing. No additional salt is used at the dry rub stage for this reason.
- **On Thick-Cut Bacon:** Thick-cut slices are ideal — they have enough mass to develop interior chew while the exterior crisps. Standard thin-cut bacon can be used but will finish significantly faster (check at 4–5 hours) and will skew toward a more brittle, chip-like result.
- **On Humidity:** Dehydrators are sensitive to ambient conditions. On humid summer days, add 1–2 hours. On dry winter days in a climate-controlled home, strips may finish closer to the 6-hour mark. Your doneness indicators are more reliable than the clock.
- **On Storage:** Once fully cooled, store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 days, or refrigerate for up to 2 weeks. Do not seal while warm — trapped steam will rehydrate the strips and destroy the texture.
- **On Garnish Use:** For plating applications, allow strips to cool fully, then cut with a sharp knife or kitchen shears into lardons, crumbles, or precise batons. The firm, dry texture holds its shape cleanly under heat-lamp conditions.
- **Running Multiple Profiles Simultaneously:** All four profiles share the same brine. Brine a full package together, then divide and rub separately before loading onto labeled trays. Rotate tray positions at the 4-hour mark for even air circulation.

## Glossary

- **Osmotic Diffusion:** The process by which dissolved compounds in a brine migrate through a semi-permeable membrane (the meat's cell walls) from an area of high concentration to low, seasoning the interior.
- **Fat Rendering:** The process of applying low, sustained heat to convert solid fat into liquid, which then drains away or is reabsorbed — producing a firmer, less greasy texture in cured meat products.
- **Gochugaru:** Korean coarse red pepper flakes. Fruity, moderately hot, and slightly smoky — a more complex and less sharp substitute for generic crushed red pepper.
- **Five Spice:** A Chinese spice blend typically containing star anise, cloves, cinnamon, Sichuan pepper, and fennel seed. Warm, aromatic, and slightly sweet.
- **Mise en Place:** "Everything in its place." The culinary practice of preparing, measuring, and organizing all ingredients and equipment before cooking begins.
