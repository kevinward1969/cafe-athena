Deeply caramelized, gelatin-rich veal stock extracted under high pressure.

**Yield:** ~2.5 Liters | **Prep:** 45 min | **Cook:** 2 hr (Pressure) | **Total:** ~4 hr

---

## Headnote

While traditional brown stock requires 8–12 hours of simmering to extract gelatin, the pressure cooker elevates the boiling point of water to ~250°F (121°C). This allows the Maillard reaction to continue even while the bones are submerged, resulting in a stock with profound depth and a darker color in a fraction of the time. **Teaching Idea: The Pincage.** To achieve the signature mahogany color of a true *fond brun*, we roast tomato paste onto the mirepoix until it turns rust-colored. This process, called *pincage* (pin-sahj), caramelizes the sugars in the tomato and reduces acidity.

---

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

- **Roast the Bones:** Preheat oven to 450°F (230°C). Arrange bones in a single layer on a sheet pan.
- **Prep the Veg:** Wash and rough chop the mirepoix (onions, carrots, celery).
- **Ready the Pot:** Ensure your pressure cooker (minimum 6–8 quart) is clean and the seal is intact.
- **Warm the Liquid:** Have the wine ready for deglazing and cold water accessible for filling.

---

## Ingredients

### The Base

- 2000 g (4.5 lbs) Veal or Beef Knuckle Bones (split to expose marrow)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) Neutral Oil (Vegetable or Grapeseed)

### The Mirepoix & Aromatics

- 300 g (2 medium) Yellow Onions, rough chop (skin on for color)
- 150 g (2 stalks) Carrots, rough chop
- 150 g (2 stalks) Celery, rough chop
- 60 g (¼ cup) Tomato Paste
- 5 g (1 tsp) Black Peppercorns, whole
- 2 g (2 leaves) Bay Leaves, fresh or dried
- 5 g (4 sprigs) Fresh Thyme

### The Liquid

- 240 ml (1 cup) Dry Red Wine (Cabernet or Merlot)
- 2500 ml (2.5 qt) Cold Water (approximate, enough to cover bones)

## Method

**Phase 1: The Roast.**

1. **Roast Bones:** Toss the bones with the neutral oil to coat. Roast in the 450°F (230°C) oven for **45–60 minutes**, turning halfway through, until they are deep dark brown.

> Why: Deep browning creates the foundation of the flavor profile via the Maillard reaction.

1. **Drain Fat:** Remove bones from the pan and transfer them to the pressure cooker pot. Discard the rendered fat from the roasting pan, but keep the brown bits (*fond*) stuck to the bottom.

**Phase 2: The Pincage & Deglaze.**

1. **Sauté Mirepoix:** In a separate skillet or directly in the pressure cooker (if it has a sauté mode), add a splash of fresh oil and the onions, carrots, and celery. Sauté until the vegetables begin to brown (about 8–10 minutes).
2. **Pincé:** Add the tomato paste to the vegetables. Stir constantly for 2–3 minutes until the paste darkens from bright red to a brick/rust color.

> Sensory Cue: The scent will change from raw tomato to sweet and roasted.

1. **Combine:** Add the caramelized vegetable mixture to the pot with the bones.
2. **Deglaze:** Place the roasting sheet (used for bones) over a burner on medium heat. Pour in the red wine. Scrape up the stuck brown bits (*fond*) with a wooden spoon or spatula.
3. **Pour:** Add this wine-fond liquid into the pressure cooker pot.

**Phase 3: Pressure Extraction.**

1. **Fill:** Cover the bones and vegetables with cold water. Do not fill past the "Max Fill" line of your pressure cooker.

> Why: Starting with cold water promotes clearer extraction of proteins.

1. **Add Aromatics:** Toss in the peppercorns, bay leaves, and thyme.
2. **Cook:** Seal the lid. Bring to **High Pressure** (15 psi). Cook for **2 hours**.
3. **Natural Release:** Turn off the heat and allow the pressure to release naturally (about 20–30 minutes). **Critical:** Do not use the "Quick Release" valve. The violent boiling caused by rapid depressurization will emulsify the fat and make the stock cloudy and greasy.

**Phase 4: Clarification & Storage.**

1. **Strain:** Carefully pour the stock through a fine-mesh sieve (chinoise) into a clean container. Do not press on the solids, or you will cloud the stock.
2. **Chill:** Place the container in an ice bath to cool rapidly to below 40°F (4°C).
3. **Defat:** Once fully chilled, the fat will form a solid cap on the surface. Lift this off with a spoon before using or freezing the stock.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

- **The Jelly Standard:** A properly made brown stock should set into a firm, wiggly jelly (meat jello) when chilled. This indicates high gelatin content, which gives sauces like Espagnole their velvety mouthfeel.
- **Salt:** Never salt a stock during production. Stock is an ingredient that will be reduced later; salting now could result in an inedibly salty sauce after reduction.
- **Remouillage:** You can reuse the bones for a "second wash" (Remouillage). Cover the used bones with water and pressure cook for another 2 hours. The result will be weaker but perfect for use as the liquid in your next batch of stock.

## Glossary

- **Fond Brun:** French for "Brown Stock."
- **Maillard Reaction:** A chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that gives browned food its distinctive flavor.
- **Pincé:** The culinary technique of cooking tomato paste until it caramelizes and turns brown.
- **Mirepoix:** A flavor base made from cooked diced vegetables, usually onions, carrots, and celery.
