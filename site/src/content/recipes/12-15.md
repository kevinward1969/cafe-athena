A structural, multi-variant foundation for baked, edible "stained glass" tiles and molded vessels built from high-protein cheese matrices and moisture-controlled inclusions.

**Yield:** 12–18 tiles/vessels | **Prep:** 45 min | **Cook:** 20 min | **Total:** 1 hr 5 min

## Headnote

This folio is the "Construction Site" of the larder — we are building structural components rather than simple garnishes. By manipulating the cheese matrix, we control the tensile strength, color, and snap of the frico, producing everything from a delicate herb-laminated tile to a rigid, high-walled vessel. Baking — rather than pan-cooking — gives even browning across a full sheet, a longer plastic-phase window for shaping, and hands-free batch control.

**Teaching Idea: Thermal Plasticity.** Between 150°F/65°C and 110°F/43°C, the frico is in its "plastic" phase — drapeable, foldable, geometrically cuttable with clean edges. Once it drops below 100°F/38°C, it enters the "glass" phase; any attempt to reshape will shatter it. Oven-baked fricos hold the plastic phase roughly 30–45 seconds on the counter (versus 5–10 seconds from a stovetop pan) because the Silpat and cheese retain heat far longer than a bare pan releases it.

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

* **Stabilize Inclusions:** Complete the **01-20 Hydro-Thermal Stabilization** process for all inclusions in advance — they must be bone-dry before you begin.
* **Matrix Prep:** Microplane all cheeses into a fluffy pile. Do not use a standard box grater; we need a high surface-area "snow" for an even, lacy melt.
* **Oven Setup:** Preheat to 375°F/190°C on Standard Bake, rack in center position. Line a full sheet pan with a Silpat or parchment.
* **Shape Station:** Arrange molds (rocks glasses, shot glasses, or cannoli forms) and metal ring/cookie cutters on a cool surface near the oven.
* **Tool Setup:** Thin offset spatula, 3-inch ring mold (as stencil), timer.

## Method

**Phase 1: Form the Discs.**

1. Place the 3-inch ring mold on the prepared Silpat as a stencil.
2. Sprinkle a thin, uniform layer of the **Cheese Matrix** (approximately 1 tbsp, ~6 g) inside the ring — thin enough that you can *just* see the Silpat through the cheese.
3. Lift the ring straight up. Repeat across the sheet, leaving at least 1 inch between discs. A full sheet holds 9–12 discs.

**Phase 2: First Bake (The Webbing).**

1. Bake at 375°F/190°C for 2–3 minutes. The cheese will melt and begin "webbing" — lace-like holes opening across the surface, edges just starting to bubble, no color yet.
2. Pull the pan and move quickly into Phase 3. The sheet will stay hot enough to keep the cheese in working condition for roughly 60 seconds.

**Phase 3: Lamination (The Sandwich).**

1. Lay your stabilized **Laminate** (herb leaf, carrot shape, cabbage julienne) flat onto each partially-melted disc, pressing gently so it contacts the cheese.
2. Dust a second, very light layer of matrix (~1 tsp) over each inclusion.
3. **Why:** Adding the laminate at the webbing stage locks it into the matrix *before* the cheese vitrifies. Added earlier, the inclusion steams and deposits moisture into the cheese (weakening the tile); added after the final render, it sits on top as a garnish rather than a structural element.

**Phase 4: Second Bake (The Render).**

1. Return the pan to the oven. Bake a further 4–5 minutes, until the cheese is uniformly golden-amber and the bubbling has slowed to a lazy, deliberate rate.
2. **Sensory Cue:** A thin offset spatula should slide cleanly under a disc, lifting the entire tile as one rigid, lace-like sheet without drooping or tearing.

**Phase 5: Shaping (The 30–45 Second Window).**

Work one disc at a time. Keep the remaining discs on the sheet pan — the hot Silpat extends your plastic phase. If a disc has cooled below working temperature, return the full sheet to the oven for 30 seconds to re-plasticize.

* **Option 1: The Flat Tile (Geometric Cut):** Lift the disc to a cool surface with an offset spatula. Within 5 seconds, punch with a metal ring or cookie cutter. Remove scraps while still warm.
* **Option 2: The Molded Vessel (Vase/Cup):** Drape the hot disc over an inverted rocks glass (large vessel) or shot glass (mini). Press a second glass gently over the top to set the pleats into a uniform cup.
* **Option 3: The Structural Tube:** Wrap the disc around a cannoli mold or clean food-grade pipe; press the seam lightly to close.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

### Oven Format Notes (Samsung)

* **Standard Bake (primary method):** 375°F/190°C, 2–3 min webbing + 4–5 min render. Most forgiving method; best for learning the plastic-phase windows and for large-batch production.
* **Convection Bake:** Drop to 350°F/175°C and shave ~1 min from each phase (1.5–2 min webbing + 3–4 min render). Forced air browns faster and more evenly across the sheet, but edges color more aggressively — rotate the pan 180° halfway through the render if the oven runs hot on one side. Best choice for a full sheet at once.
* **Air Fryer Mode:** 350°F/175°C, 90 sec webbing + 2–3 min render. Cut parchment to fit the basket and work in small batches of 2–3 discs. Rapid convection cooks fast and can over-brown edges; pull the moment active bubbling slows. Best for à la minute service when you need three tiles for one plate.

### Technique Notes

* **Carrot Geometric Shapes:** To mimic the look of the basil in reference images, punch small aspic shapes from mandolined carrot slices *before* blanching.
* **Flatness Protocol:** For perfectly flat tiles, place a second Silpat and a weighted sheet pan on top of the fricos as they cool through the glass phase.
* **Flavor Stacking:** Mimolette (Variant C) pairs exceptionally well with purple cabbage for high-contrast visual and earthy flavor.
* **Scrap Utility:** Do not discard geometric off-cuts — they provide a "broken glass" garnish with concentrated umami.

## Glossary

* **Hydro-Thermal Stabilization:** The combined use of heat and pressure to prep inclusions for fat-based cooking.
* **Thermal Plasticity:** The state in which cheese fat is pliable before crystallization.
* **Lamination:** Layering material (cheese–inclusion–cheese) to create a reinforced composite structure.
* **Matrix:** The structural medium (cheese) that holds the laminates.
* **Silpat:** A fiberglass-reinforced silicone baking mat (genericized brand name) used to line sheet pans. Non-stick, reusable, heat-stable to ~480°F/249°C — preferred over parchment for baked fricos because clean release protects fragile lace structure and the mat retains counter heat long enough to extend the plastic-phase shaping window.
* **Webbing:** The initial melt stage at which the cheese forms a lace-like, partially-set network — the correct window in which to introduce the laminate.
