A competition-grade beef braise built on cold-smoke, deep Maillard sear, and a layered umami foundation, finished with a glossy beurre noisette-mounted jus.

**Yield:** 6–8 servings | **Prep:** 30 min | **Inactive:** 45 min (cold smoke) | **Cook:** ~5 hr | **Total:** ~6 hr

## Headnote

Chuck roast is collagen-rich, connective tissue-dense shoulder muscle — one of the most forgiving and rewarding cuts in the butcher case when treated correctly. By cold-smoking before searing, we introduce a layered smoke complexity that reads as depth, not barbecue. A two-stage cook — high-pressure first, dedicated slow cooker second — gives us rapid collagen conversion followed by a gentle redistribution pass, landing the beef in that precise competition window: chunked, structurally intact, but yielding under the slightest pressure. **Teaching Idea: Glutamate Stacking.** Tomato paste, anchovy paste, and soy sauce each contain free glutamates — the compounds responsible for umami. Used together in measured amounts, they don't taste like any individual ingredient; they create a savory resonance that makes judges lean in for a second bite.

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

* **Chunk the roast:** Cut the chuck into 3–4 large, even pieces (roughly 250–300 g each). Pat completely dry with paper towels.
* **Season aggressively:** Coat all surfaces with Diamond Crystal kosher salt and cracked black pepper immediately before cold smoking.
* **Prep the aromatics:** Halve the onion, rough-chop the celery, and cut the carrots into 2-inch pieces. Peel garlic cloves, leave whole.
* **Cut the potatoes:** Quarter into even pieces, hold in cold water to prevent oxidation.
* **Build the bouquet garni:** Tie thyme, rosemary, bay leaf, and parsley stems with kitchen twine.
* **Measure and stage:** Tomato paste, anchovy paste, Worcestershire, soy sauce, Cognac, and stock should all be measured and at station before the sauté begins.
* **Prep the noisette:** Ensure your solidified beurre noisette (ref: 10-22) is at room temperature — soft but not liquid — before the jus reduction begins.

## Ingredients

### The Beef

* 1.9 kg (4.16 lb) Bone-out Chuck Roast, cut into 3–4 large chunks
* Diamond Crystal Kosher Salt (aggressive seasoning — approx. 8–10 g)
* 5 g (1 tsp) Whole Black Peppercorns, cracked

### The Aromatic Base

* 1 Large Yellow Onion, halved and rough-chopped
* 3 stalks Celery, rough-chopped
* 30 ml (2 tbsp) Neutral Oil (for searing)
* 45 g (3 tbsp) Tomato Paste
* 8 g (1 tsp) Anchovy Paste
* 15 ml (1 tbsp) Worcestershire Sauce
* 15 ml (1 tbsp) Low-Sodium Soy Sauce
* 60 ml (2 oz) Cognac (for deglazing)

### The Braise Liquid

* 720 ml (3 cups) Beef Stock (high-quality store-bought)
* 1 Bouquet Garni (thyme, rosemary, bay leaf, parsley stems)
* 6–8 Garlic Cloves, whole and peeled

### The Vegetables (Second Pass)

* 4 Medium Carrots, cut into 2-inch pieces
* 600 g (approx. 1.3 lb) Yukon Gold Potatoes, quartered

### The Jus Finish

* 60 g (4 tbsp) Solidified Beurre Noisette, room temperature (ref: 10-22)
* 10 ml (2 tsp) Red Wine Vinegar
* 15 g (1 tbsp) Flat-Leaf Parsley, rough-chopped (finishing)
* 5 g (1 tsp) Fresh Thyme Leaves (finishing)

## Method

### Phase 1 – Cold Smoke

Set up your cold smoker with your chosen wood (see Wood Selection Chart below). Arrange the seasoned beef chunks on the smoker rack with space between each piece. Cold smoke for **40–45 minutes**.

**Sensory Cue:** The surface of the beef should take on a faint golden-pink hue and carry a visible dry surface. You are not cooking the meat — surface temperature should remain below 90°F/32°C.

| Wood | Smoke Intensity | Flavor Note | Verdict |
|------|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **Cherry** | Mild | Sweet, fruity, mahogany color on bark | ⭐ Recommended |
| **Oak** | Medium | Clean, neutral, classical beef pairing | Strong alternative |
| **Apple** | Mild | Delicate, slightly sweet | Safe/subtle choice |
| **Hickory** | Heavy | Bacon-forward, aggressive | Avoid — dominates stack |
| **Mesquite** | Very Heavy | Sharp, earthy, Texas BBQ | Avoid — fights aromatics |

### Phase 2 – Sear & Build the Fond

Set the Instant Pot to **Sauté – More**. Add neutral oil and heat until shimmering — just at the edge of smoking.

Sear the smoked beef chunks **in batches**, leaving space between each piece. Sear each surface for **2–3 minutes undisturbed** until a deep mahogany crust forms. Do not crowd the pot.

**Sensory Cue:** Each face should release cleanly from the pot when properly seared. Forced removal means it needs more time. The fond on the pot bottom should be deep brown, not black.

Remove seared beef and set aside. Do not discard the fond.

### Phase 3 – Aromatic Foundation

Add onion and celery to the pot. Sauté **4–5 minutes**, scraping up the fond as the vegetables release moisture.

Add the tomato paste and anchovy paste. Cook dry, stirring constantly, for **3–4 minutes** until the paste deepens to brick-red and begins catching on the bottom.

**Sensory Cue:** The aroma shifts from raw tomato to roasted, slightly nutty. This is the Maillard reaction occurring in the paste — do not rush this step.

Add the Worcestershire and soy sauce. Stir to combine. Add the Cognac and deglaze, scraping every bit of fond from the bottom of the pot.

**Sensory Cue:** The alcohol will volatilize rapidly with a sharp, rising aroma. Cook 60–90 seconds until the sharp edge dissipates.

### Phase 4 – First Pass (Pressure)

Return the seared beef chunks to the pot. Add stock, whole garlic cloves, cracked peppercorns, and the bouquet garni. Liquid should reach approximately halfway up the beef — do not fully submerge.

Seal the Instant Pot. Set to **Pressure Cook – High** for **55 minutes**. Allow a **natural pressure release for 20 minutes**, then carefully vent remaining pressure.

**Sensory Cue:** The braising liquid should have deepened considerably in color. The beef should show significant tenderness but still hold its chunk shape — not yet falling apart.

Remove the beef and set aside, covered loosely with foil. Discard the bouquet garni. Strain and reserve the braising liquid.

### Phase 5 – Second Pass (Slow Cooker)

Transfer the beef chunks to your slow cooker. Add the carrots and potatoes. Pour the strained braising liquid over, ensuring the vegetables are mostly submerged.

Set to **Low**. Cook for **3–4 hours**.

**Sensory Cue:** At 3 hours, test a chunk of beef with light pressure from a spoon. It should yield and break cleanly without disintegrating. Carrots should be completely tender with no resistance. Potatoes should hold their shape but cut cleanly.

Remove the beef and vegetables with a slotted spoon. Set aside, covered.

### Phase 6 – Jus Reduction & Mount

Transfer the braising liquid to a wide saucepan over medium-high heat. Reduce by approximately half — **15–20 minutes** — until the jus coats the back of a spoon (nappé consistency) and carries a glossy sheen.

Remove from heat. Allow the jus to cool for 90 seconds — it should still be hot but no longer violently boiling.

Add the solidified beurre noisette (ref: 10-22) in small pieces, whisking constantly in a circular motion. Add the red wine vinegar. Taste and adjust salt.

**Sensory Cue:** The jus should ribbon off the whisk and coat a spoon with a clean, glossy layer. A finger drawn across the spoon should leave a clean line that holds (nappé). If the jus breaks and looks greasy, the liquid was too hot — pull it further off heat and whisk vigorously.

### Phase 7 – Plate

Return the beef and vegetables briefly to the jus to warm through and glaze, 2–3 minutes over low heat. Plate the beef chunks with vegetables. Spoon jus generously over the top. Finish with flat-leaf parsley and fresh thyme leaves.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

* **Dry Brine Option (Recommended for Planned Cooks):** For maximum flavor penetration and a superior bark, salt the beef chunks 12–24 hours ahead and refrigerate uncovered. The salt draws moisture to the surface, then re-absorbs, seasoning the meat deeply. Resume at Phase 1 (cold smoke) when ready. This single step elevates the result significantly at competition level.
* **On the Anchovy Paste:** Do not skip or substitute. It dissolves completely under heat and contributes pure glutamate depth with zero identifiable fish flavor. This is a classical Italian braising technique widely used in professional kitchens.
* **Jus Mount Temperature is Critical:** The beurre noisette emulsification requires that the jus be hot but not boiling. Above approximately 185°F/85°C, the butter fat breaks before it can emulsify. When in doubt, pull further off heat and work quickly.
* **Cognac Substitution:** Dry sherry is the closest alternative. Brandy is acceptable. Do not substitute bourbon — the vanilla and oak notes will conflict with the aromatic stack.
* **Vegetable Timing:** The two-stage cook is designed to keep the vegetables structurally intact. Adding them at the slow cooker pass rather than the pressure pass prevents complete breakdown. If you want softer vegetables, add 30–45 minutes to the slow cook time.

## Glossary

* **Bouquet Garni:** A bundle of fresh herbs tied with twine and added to braises or stocks. The bundle form allows easy removal before serving.
* **Cold Smoking:** Exposing food to smoke at temperatures below 90°F/32°C. Adds flavor without cooking the product.
* **Fond:** The caramelized proteins and sugars that adhere to the bottom of a pan during searing. The flavor foundation of any pan sauce or braise.
* **Glutamate:** An amino acid responsible for the umami taste sensation. Present in tomato paste, anchovy paste, soy sauce, aged cheeses, and mushrooms.
* **Maillard Reaction:** A chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that produces the complex flavors and brown color associated with searing and roasting. Requires surface temperatures above 280°F/140°C.
* **Nappé:** The consistency at which a sauce coats the back of a spoon and a clean line holds when a finger is drawn through it.
