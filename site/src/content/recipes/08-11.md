Blanch-and-roast Brussels sprouts, caramelized cut-side down, finished with a sharp, butter-mounted honey-mustard pan sauce.

**Yield:** 4 servings | **Prep:** 15 min | **Cook:** 35 min | **Total:** 50 min

---

## Headnote

Brussels sprouts are among the most technique-sensitive vegetables in the kitchen: roast them wrong and you get sulfurous, chewy globes; roast them right and you get deeply caramelized, nutty, and tender-crisp halves that can anchor a plate. The sequence here — blanch, shock, dry, roast cut-side down — is deliberate at every step. A brief blanch reduces the concentration of glucosinolates, the sulfur compounds responsible for the vegetable's harsh edge, while simultaneously locking the chlorophyll into a vivid green. Drying the surface before roasting is not optional: residual moisture will steam the sprouts rather than brown them, robbing you of the Maillard crust that makes this dish worth eating. The warm honey-mustard pan sauce is built directly in the roasting pan after the sprouts come out — a two-minute technique that leverages every caramelized bit left behind.

**Teaching Idea: Blanch-Shock-Dry.** Blanching in aggressively salted boiling water and shocking in ice water is a three-outcome technique: (1) it drives the harsh glucosinolates into the water, mellowing the vegetable's flavor; (2) it fixes chlorophyll in a bright green state before the enzyme chlorophyllase has time to degrade it; (3) it partially hydrates the vegetable, so the oven's job shifts from cooking through to crisping the surface. The drying step after shocking is equally critical — water on the surface vaporizes at 212°F/100°C, which means a wet sprout will spend the first 10 minutes of roasting time steaming in its own moisture rather than browning.

## Ingredients

### The Sprouts

- 680 g (1½ lb) Brussels sprouts, medium-sized (roughly golf-ball diameter)
- Diamond Crystal kosher salt, for blanching water (approximately 10 g per liter)

### The Roast

- 30 ml (2 tbsp) neutral oil (grapeseed or canola)
- 3 g (½ tsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt
- 1 g (¼ tsp) freshly ground black pepper

### The Honey-Mustard Pan Sauce

- 1 medium shallot, finely minced (approximately 40 g)
- 60 ml (¼ cup) dry white wine or dry vermouth
- 60 ml (¼ cup) chicken stock or vegetable stock
- 20 g (1 tbsp) whole-grain Dijon mustard
- 15 g (2 tsp) honey
- 10 ml (2 tsp) apple cider vinegar
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter, cold and cubed
- 1 g (⅛ tsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt
- Flaky sea salt (Maldon) and freshly ground black pepper, for finishing

---

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

- **Trim** the stem end of each Brussels sprout flush with the base and remove any yellowed outer leaves.
- **Halve** each sprout through the stem end so both halves retain their core (which holds the leaves together).
- **Prepare an ice bath** — a large bowl filled with cold water and ice cubes.
- **Cube** the cold butter for the pan sauce and return it to the refrigerator.
- **Mince** the shallot finely.
- **Measure and combine** the stock and wine in a small vessel, ready to add together.
- **Preheat the oven** to 450°F/230°C with a rimmed sheet pan inside — the hot pan is essential for an immediate contact sear on the cut face.
- **Line a second sheet pan or clean kitchen towels** for drying the blanched sprouts.

---

## Method

**Phase 1: Blanch and Shock.** Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Salt it aggressively — the water should taste noticeably of the sea (approximately 10 g Diamond Crystal kosher salt per liter). Add the halved Brussels sprouts and blanch for exactly 3 minutes; they should be bright green and barely yielding at the stem but not cooked through.

Drain immediately and transfer to the ice bath. Agitate them briefly to halt cooking. Leave in the ice bath for 2 minutes, then drain thoroughly.

**Why:** Three minutes of blanching is enough to reduce glucosinolate concentration by approximately 30–40% and fix chlorophyll without fully cooking the vegetable. Longer blanching begins to leach the color out and overcooks the outer leaves.

**Phase 2: Dry the Sprouts.** Spread the blanched, shocked sprouts in a single layer on clean kitchen towels or a sheet pan lined with paper towels. Pat them dry with additional towels. Let them sit at room temperature for at least 5 minutes.

**Sensory Cue:** The cut surfaces should look matte and dry, not shiny or damp. Any visible moisture means more time is needed. This is the most overlooked step — do not rush it.

**Phase 3: Season and Roast.** Remove the preheated sheet pan from the oven using heavy oven mitts. Working quickly, toss the dried sprouts with the neutral oil, salt, and pepper directly on the hot pan, then arrange them cut-side down in a single layer. They should sizzle audibly on contact with the hot metal.

Return the pan to the 450°F/230°C oven. Roast without disturbing for 20–22 minutes.

**Sensory Cue:** The cut faces should be deeply caramelized — dark amber to mahogany brown — with the outer leaves showing char at the tips. A flat spatula slid under a sprout should meet little resistance; the crust should release cleanly. If the cut face is still pale, return to the oven for 3–4 additional minutes. Do not turn them during roasting.

**Phase 4: Build the Pan Sauce.** Transfer the roasted sprouts to a serving vessel and tent loosely with foil. Set the roasting pan directly over a burner (or transfer the drippings and any caramelized bits to a small skillet) over medium heat. Add the minced shallot to the pan and cook for 1–2 minutes, stirring, until the shallot softens and turns translucent in the residual fat.

**Sensory Cue:** The shallot should sizzle immediately on contact. If there is insufficient fat in the pan from the roasting, add a small drizzle of neutral oil.

**Phase 5: Deglaze and Reduce.** Pour in the wine and stock together. Using a wooden spoon or heatproof spatula, scrape the caramelized fond from the pan — this is the flavor base of the sauce. Bring to a rapid simmer and reduce until the liquid is approximately halved and slightly syrupy, about 2–3 minutes.

**Sensory Cue:** The sauce should coat the back of a spoon lightly. If you drag a line through the coating, it should hold for 2–3 seconds before flowing back together.

**Phase 6: Finish the Sauce.** Remove the pan from the heat. Whisk in the whole-grain mustard, honey, and apple cider vinegar. Return to low heat briefly if needed to bring to a gentle simmer, then remove from heat again. Add the cold butter cubes one or two at a time, swirling and whisking constantly until each cube is fully emulsified before adding the next.

**Sensory Cue:** The sauce should turn glossy and visibly thicker as each cube of butter emulsifies. If the butter breaks (the sauce becomes greasy and the fat separates), the pan is too hot — remove from heat entirely and continue swirling with the residual heat. Season with the kosher salt and a few turns of black pepper.

**Why:** Mounting with cold butter (monter au beurre) off-heat creates a stable, glossy emulsion. Cold butter added to a sauce that is too hot will melt before it can emulsify, resulting in a greasy, broken sauce rather than a silky one.

**Phase 7: Plate.** Arrange the roasted sprouts on the serving vessel, cut-side up so the caramelized faces are visible. Spoon the warm honey-mustard sauce over and around the sprouts. Finish with a pinch of flaky sea salt.

## Variations

### Vegan Version

Replace the butter in the pan sauce with 30 g (2 tbsp) of cold extra virgin olive oil and use vegetable stock. Substitute 15 g (1 tbsp) of maple syrup for the honey. The sauce will be thinner and less glossy (it will not emulsify the same way) — reduce slightly more aggressively before adding the oil. Dietary qualification: Vegan, Gluten-Free.

## Chef's Notes

- **Sprout size matters:** Aim for uniformity — medium, golf-ball-sized sprouts (about 30–40 g each). Very large sprouts take longer and risk charring on the outside before the core is tender; very small sprouts overcook quickly. If your bag contains mixed sizes, halve the large ones and leave the small ones whole to approximate even cooking times.
- **The hot pan start:** Placing sprouts on a cold pan and then putting it in the oven is the most common mistake with roasted vegetables. The preheated pan gives you an immediate contact sear on the cut face, mimicking the effect of a hot griddle. Without it, the sprouts begin releasing moisture before the surface temperature is high enough to brown.
- **Make-ahead:** Blanch and shock the sprouts up to 24 hours ahead; store dried in an airtight container in the refrigerator. The pan sauce must be made fresh — do not hold it for more than 15 minutes or the emulsion will break.
- **Wine substitution:** If you prefer not to cook with wine, replace with an equal volume of additional stock plus 5 ml (1 tsp) of additional apple cider vinegar for acidity.
- **Fond:** If the roasting pan produces heavy fond (very dark residue), deglaze carefully — some char is flavor, but excessive blackened bits will make the sauce bitter. Use judgment: golden-to-amber fond is an asset; black is not.

## Glossary

- Glucosinolate: A class of sulfur-containing compounds found in Brassica vegetables (Brussels sprouts, broccoli, cabbage). When cells are damaged by cutting or heat, glucosinolates break down into pungent sulfur compounds. Blanching partially dissolves them into the cooking water, mellowing the vegetable's flavor.
- Maillard Reaction: The chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that produces brown color and complex roasted flavors when food is exposed to sufficient dry heat. Requires surface temperatures above approximately 280°F/140°C and a dry surface — surface moisture suppresses it.
- Chlorophyll: The green pigment in plants. Chlorophyll is heat-sensitive; brief blanching and immediate shocking in ice water fixes it in the bright green state before the enzyme chlorophyllase degrades it to drab olive tones.
- Fond: The caramelized residue left on the bottom of a pan after searing or roasting. Fond is concentrated with Maillard compounds and Maillard-related flavor precursors; deglazing with liquid dissolves it into the sauce.
- Monter au Beurre: "To mount with butter." The technique of whisking cold cubed butter into a hot sauce off-heat to create a stable, glossy emulsion. Cold butter emulsifies; warm or room-temperature butter melts out and breaks the sauce.
- Emulsify: To combine two immiscible liquids (here, water-based pan juices and fat from the butter) into a stable, homogeneous suspension. The natural lecithin in butter milk solids acts as an emulsifier, but the technique — off-heat, constant agitation, cold fat — is what keeps it stable.
