A refined expression of the Burgundian classic using a "clean braise," large-format beef, and overnight maturation for a glass-like sauce.

**Yield:** 4 servings | **Prep:** 60 min | **Inactive:** 12–24 hr | **Cook:** 4 hr | **Total:** 17–29 hr

## Headnote

This MasterClass iteration elevates the rustic stew to a technical exercise by separating the beef from its braising liquid for a clean finish. Rather than stewing small cubes into oblivion, we use large 2-inch portions to preserve internal moisture and texture, then strain the liquid free of spent vegetables and reduce it independently to a glass-like sheen. **Teaching Idea: The Maturation Cycle.** Braises are chemically superior the day after cooking. As the meat cools in its liquid, the muscle fibers relax and act like a sponge, drawing the seasoned sauce back into the center of the beef. Reheating the next day continues to gelatinize collagen without further toughening the proteins — the result is meat that is deeply seasoned all the way through, not just on the surface.

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

- **Beef prep:** Trim the chuck of hard fat and cut into large 2-inch (5 cm) cubes. Pat extremely dry with paper towels.
- **Aromatic prep:** Brunoise the carrots, celery, and shallots. Mince the garlic.
- **Garnish prep:** Clean the mushrooms (halve if large), peel the pearl onions, slice the slab bacon into ¼-inch lardons.
- **Bouquet garni:** Tie the thyme, rosemary, and bay leaves together with kitchen twine.
- **Cartouche:** Cut a circle of parchment the same diameter as your Dutch oven.

## Ingredients

### The Braise

- 1.2 kg (2.6 lb) beef chuck roast, cut into 2-inch cubes
- 30 g (2 tbsp) neutral oil or clarified butter
- 150 g (1 cup) carrots, finely diced
- 100 g (¾ cup) celery, finely diced
- 120 g (¾ cup) shallots, finely diced
- 20 g (4 cloves) garlic, minced
- 30 g (3 tbsp) all-purpose flour
- 750 ml (1 bottle) dry red wine (Pinot Noir or Burgundy)
- 1 L (4 cups) high-quality beef stock
- 15 g (1 tbsp) tomato paste
- 1 bouquet garni (thyme, rosemary, 2 bay leaves)
- 10 g (1½ tsp) Morton kosher salt, plus more to taste
- 3 g (1 tsp) freshly cracked black pepper

### The Garnish

- 200 g (7 oz) slab bacon, cut into lardons
- 250 g (8 oz) pearl onions, peeled
- 300 g (10 oz) cremini mushrooms, cleaned and halved
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 5 g (2 tbsp) fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

## Method

**Phase 1: The Pinçage and Fond.** In a large Dutch oven over medium-high heat, add the oil. Working in batches to avoid overcrowding, sear the beef cubes for 3–4 minutes per side until a deep, dark crust forms (Maillard reaction). Remove beef and set aside. Reduce heat to medium and add the carrots, celery, and shallots to the rendered fat. Sauté for 6–8 minutes until softened and the shallots are translucent. Add the garlic and cook for 1 minute until fragrant. Stir in the tomato paste and cook for 2–3 minutes, stirring constantly, until the paste turns a deep rusty red and begins to stick slightly to the bottom — this is the *pinçage*. Sprinkle the flour over the vegetables and stir for 2 more minutes to cook out the raw flour taste (the *singer*).

**Phase 2: The Clean Braise.** Pour in the red wine and use a wooden spoon to scrape all the browned bits (fond) from the bottom of the pot. Bring to a simmer and reduce the liquid by half, about 10–12 minutes. Return the beef and any accumulated juices to the pot. Add the beef stock until the meat is just submerged, then add the bouquet garni, salt, and pepper. Place the parchment cartouche directly onto the surface of the liquid, cover with the lid, and transfer to a preheated 300°F/150°C oven. Braise for 3–3.5 hours. **Sensory cue:** the beef is ready when a paring knife slides in and out of a cube with zero resistance.

**Phase 3: The Maturation (Overnight).** Remove the pot from the oven. Allow it to cool at room temperature for 1 hour, then refrigerate the entire pot overnight (12–24 hours).

**Phase 4: The Finish.** Remove the pot from the refrigerator. Lift off and discard the solidified orange fat cap from the surface. Gently remove the beef cubes and set aside. Strain the braising liquid through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean saucepan, discarding the spent vegetables and bouquet garni. Bring the liquid to a simmer over medium heat and reduce until the sauce is thick enough to coat the back of a spoon (*nappé*) and has a glass-like sheen. In a separate skillet, render the bacon lardons until crispy. Remove the bacon but leave the fat behind. Add the pearl onions and mushrooms to the bacon fat with the butter and sauté for 8–10 minutes until golden brown and tender. Return the beef and garnishes to the reduced sauce. Simmer gently for 5–10 minutes until the beef is heated through. Garnish with fresh parsley before serving.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

- **The Wine.** Never cook with a wine you wouldn't drink. The acidity and tannins concentrate during reduction; a harsh wine produces a harsh sauce. A drinkable village-level Burgundy or a quality California Pinot is the right call.
- **The Cut.** If chuck roast is unavailable, beef cheeks provide an even higher gelatin content for a silkier sauce. Adjust braise time down by 30 minutes — cheeks are denser and heat through faster than cubed chuck.
- **Why the Cartouche.** A parchment cartouche slows evaporation and keeps the surface of the braise just moist enough to prevent the exposed tops of the beef from drying out, while still allowing some reduction — a tighter seal than a lid alone.
- **Companion Recipe.** This is the elevated cousin of **07-04 Beef Bourguignon (The Elevated Short Rib Braise)**. Choose 07-04 for a one-day rustic version; choose this for a planned, multi-day Fine Dining service.

## Glossary

- **Pinçage:** The deliberate browning of tomato paste in fat to convert raw acidity into caramelized, glutamate-rich umami.
- **Cartouche:** A circular parchment lid placed directly on the surface of a braise to slow evaporation and keep the food moist.
- **Singer:** Dusting ingredients with flour inside the cooking vessel to create a roux-like thickening base in place.
- **Fond:** The browned bits of protein and sugar left on the bottom of a searing vessel — a concentrated source of flavor unlocked by deglazing.
- **Nappé:** The consistency of a sauce thick enough to coat the back of a spoon evenly.
- **Lardons:** Thick batons of cured pork (usually slab bacon), rendered for fat and used as a textural garnish.
