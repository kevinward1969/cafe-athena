A boldly layered dry rub built on smoked paprika, turbinado sugar, and sumac — balanced, heat-free, and ready to anchor anything from your grill grate to your smoker box.

**Yield:** ~240 g (approx. ¾ cup + 2 tbsp) | **Prep:** 10 min | **Cook:** 0 min | **Total:** 10 min

---

## Headnote

This rub was reverse-engineered from memory — a blend encountered at a beach-front grill near Coronado Island that left a specific impression: smoke-forward, faintly sweet, with a bright lift that cut through rendered fat without heat. The architecture here is deliberate: smoked paprika as the primary volume spice, turbinado sugar for caramelization and crust formation rather than sweetness, sumac for the acid lift that citric acid handles less elegantly, and dried porcini powder as an invisible umami anchor that you won't identify by name but will absolutely miss if it's absent. The result is a building-block rub — protein-agnostic, calibrated for balance, and designed to be the foundation other blends reference.

**Teaching Idea: Flavor Stacking.** A dry rub functions as a layered system, not a single flavor. Volume spices (paprika) provide the base mass and color; umami components (porcini) deepen perceived savoriness without adding a specific flavor note; acid (sumac) adds brightness that reads as freshness; sugar drives the Maillard reaction and caramelization at the grill surface; salt controls moisture and seasoning across the cook. Understanding which ingredient is doing which job lets you adjust any layer independently — more smoke, more lift, more crust — without collapsing the whole formula.

## Ingredients

**The Rub**

- 36 g (2 tbsp + 2 tsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt
- 18 g (1 tbsp + 1½ tsp) turbinado sugar
- 72 g (¼ cup + 2 tbsp) smoked paprika
- 36 g (3 tbsp) granulated onion
- 24 g (2½ tbsp) granulated garlic
- 18 g (3 tbsp) dried porcini powder (or mushroom powder)
- 18 g (2 tbsp) sumac
- 12 g (1 tbsp + 1 tsp) ground black pepper
- 6 g (2 tsp) ground cumin
- 1.2 g (¼ tsp) ground cloves

## Mise en Place

- Measure all ingredients into individual small bowls or prep cups before combining.
- Confirm porcini powder is fully dry — any moisture will cause clumping during storage.
- If grinding your own cloves from whole, grind fine and measure after grinding.

## Method

**Phase 1: Combine the Dry Blend**

In a medium mixing bowl, combine all measured ingredients. Whisk firmly for 60–90 seconds, reaching the bottom of the bowl throughout, until the blend is uniform in color — a deep brick-red with no visible white salt pockets or dark clove flecks concentrated in one area.

**Why:** Granulated onion and garlic are slightly coarser than fine-ground spices and tend to settle. Thorough whisking ensures even distribution, which matters at application — you want every pinch of rub pulling the same flavor ratio.

**Phase 2: Check Texture and Flow**

Pinch a small amount between your fingers. The rub should feel dry and free-flowing with very slight graininess from the turbinado. If it clumps, the porcini powder may have absorbed ambient moisture — spread the blend on a dry sheet pan and air-dry at room temperature for 20–30 minutes before storing.

**Phase 3: Store**

Transfer to an airtight jar. Label with the date. The rub is ready to use immediately.

## Chef's Notes

**Heat Upgrade (Add to Base or Make a Separate Hot Build)**

The base rub carries no chili heat by design — the smoked paprika is sweet or bittersweet, not hot. To add heat, incorporate one of the following into the full batch:

- Ancho chili powder: 12–18 g (1–1½ tbsp) — dark fruit, mild, slow warmth. The most complementary choice.
- Chipotle powder: 6–9 g (½–¾ tbsp) — adds smoke-on-smoke layering with a sharper heat. Use at half the ancho quantity to avoid domination.
- Cayenne: 2–4 g (¼–½ tsp) — clean, direct heat with no competing flavor. Add sparingly and taste as you go.

All three can be combined at reduced quantities for a complex heat build. Document your ratios if you find a version you want to repeat.

**Dry-Blooming (Optional Technique Upgrade)**

For an intensified aromatic profile, bloom the cumin and cloves before adding them to the blend: heat a dry skillet over medium heat, add just the cumin and cloves, and toast 45–60 seconds until fragrant, stirring constantly. Cool completely on a plate before adding to the rub. Do not toast the paprika or porcini — paprika scorches and porcini powder is too fine-grained to toast without burning. The blooming step adds noticeable depth but requires an extra step and kills the "zero-cook" simplicity of the base method.

**Storage and Shelf Life**

Store in an airtight glass jar away from heat and direct light. The rub will hold full potency for 3 months; flavor begins to fade after 6. Turbinado sugar resists clumping better than brown sugar in this context, but high humidity environments may still cause some hardening — break apart with a fork before use.

**Application Guidance**

This rub is protein-agnostic. Apply at a rate of 2–3 g per 100 g of protein (roughly 1 tsp per 6 oz). Allow the rub to sit on the surface for a minimum of 30 minutes at room temperature before cooking — this gives the salt time to draw surface moisture and begin a light brine-in-place effect. For a deeper set, apply the night before and refrigerate uncovered. For Ch. 7 application context (beef, pork, lamb), see the relevant protein folios in The Butcher — rub behavior varies across fat content and cook temperature.

## Glossary

- Maillard reaction: A non-enzymatic browning reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that occurs above approximately 280°F/140°C, producing complex flavor compounds and dark color at seared or grilled surfaces. Distinct from caramelization, which involves only sugar.
- Caramelization: The thermal decomposition of sugars at high temperature, producing brown color and complex flavor compounds. Turbinado sugar caramelizes at the grill surface to form a set crust.
- Blooming: The process of briefly heating whole or ground spices in a dry pan or warm fat to volatilize essential oils and amplify aromatic compounds before incorporation into a dish.
- Porcini powder: A fine-ground dried porcini mushroom product used as an umami intensifier. High in free glutamates; functions as a flavor-depth anchor without contributing an identifiable mushroom note in small quantities.
- Sumac: A ground dried berry from the Rhus coriaria shrub, used across Middle Eastern and Mediterranean cooking for its tart, fruity acid contribution. pH-active; brightens and lifts perceived flavor in blended applications.
- Granulated onion: Coarse-ground dried onion with a particle size larger than onion powder. Preferred in dry rubs for slower rehydration and more even crust formation.
- Granulated garlic: Coarse-ground dried garlic, analogous to granulated onion. Provides allium depth without the sharp rawness of garlic powder.
- Flavor stacking: A design methodology in which each ingredient in a blend is assigned a functional layer (volume, umami, acid, heat, salt, sugar) so that adjustments to any layer can be made independently without destabilizing the overall formula.
