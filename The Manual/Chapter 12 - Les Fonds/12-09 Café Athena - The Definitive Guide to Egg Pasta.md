# Café Athena - The Definitive Guide to Egg Pasta

A master foundation dough yielding silky, golden pasta with a tender bite and clean egg flavor — the cornerstone of the Italian kitchen.

**Yield:** ~4 portions | **Prep:** 20 min | **Inactive:** 30 min | **Total:** ~50 min (plus rolling)

---

## Headnote

This is not a recipe — it is a skill. Egg pasta dough is one of the few preparations in the kitchen where your hands are the only real measuring tool that matters. The formula is fixed; the feel is learned. Every batch teaches you something the last one did not.

**Teaching Idea: Gluten Development.** When flour and water combine and are worked mechanically, the proteins glutenin and gliadin bond to form gluten — an elastic network that gives pasta its structure and chew. The rest period after mixing is not optional; it is when that network relaxes enough to be rolled thin without springing back.

---

## Mise en Place (Action Checklist)

- **Weigh the flour** and set aside in a mound or bowl.
- **Crack 2 whole eggs and 3 yolks** into a bowl or measuring cup and weigh them. Note that weight. Subtract it from 285 g — that is how much water to add. Combine eggs and water; whisk briefly to marry, not to aerate.
- **If mixing by hand:** clear a large work surface and have a bench scraper ready.
- **If using a food processor or stand mixer:** have the machine ready before you begin.
- Set out plastic wrap for resting.

---

## Ingredients

### The Dough

- 300 g (2 ½ cups) 00 flour or King Arthur Bread Flour
- 2 large eggs (approximately 100 g)
- 3 large egg yolks (approximately 54 g)
- water — weigh your eggs, subtract from 285 g, add that amount of water
- 1 pinch Diamond Crystal kosher salt

### For the Boil

- 6 quarts (5.7 L) water
- 65 g (3 ½ tbsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt

## Method

**Phase 1: Mixing**

**Option A — By Hand (The Well Method)**

Form the flour into a mound on your work surface. Create a wide, deep well in the center — wide enough to hold all wet ingredients without breaking. Pour the egg and water mixture into the well along with the salt. Using a fork, begin beating the wet ingredients while slowly dragging flour in from the inner walls. Work from the inside out — do not collapse the walls too quickly or the liquid will escape. Once too thick for a fork, use your hands and bench scraper to bring the dough together. Knead using the ball-roll-turn method for 8–10 minutes until smooth and cohesive.

> **Sensory Cue:** The dough should feel like warm Play-Doh — smooth, supple, not sticky, not cracking at the edges.

**Option B — Food Processor**

Add the flour first and pulse once to aerate. With the machine running, pour the egg mixture through the feed tube. Process until the dough just comes together into a shaggy mass. Scrape every bit onto your work surface and knead by hand using the ball-roll-turn method for an additional 5 minutes.

**Option C — Stand Mixer**

Add the wet ingredients to the bowl first, whisking briefly to combine. Add the flour and salt. Mix on medium-low with the dough hook for 8–10 minutes. Scrape all bits from the bowl and hook back into the dough. Finish by hand using the ball-roll-turn method for 2 minutes.

> **If the dough fights you:** Wrap in plastic and rest for 15 minutes. This allows the gluten to relax. Finish kneading after the rest.

> **If the dough is sticky:** Add flour 1 tsp at a time, working it in fully between additions.

---

**Phase 2: Rest**

Form the dough into a smooth ball. Wrap tightly in plastic wrap — no exposed surfaces. Rest at room temperature for 30 minutes.

> **Why:** The rest relaxes the gluten network developed during kneading. Without it, the dough will spring back when rolled and fight every thickness setting.

---

**Phase 3: Rolling (KitchenAid Pasta Roller Attachment)**

Divide the rested dough into 4 equal portions. Keep all portions not being worked wrapped in plastic — they dry out quickly on contact with air.

Working one portion at a time, flatten into a rough rectangle no wider than the pasta roller opening.

**Step 1 — Lamination Pass:** Set the roller to its thickest setting. Pass the dough through 2–3 times. Fold the dough — first a half fold, then in thirds like a letter as it becomes more pliable. This builds structure and drives you toward a clean rectangle.

**Step 2 — Progressive Thinning:** Move through the settings one number at a time. Do not skip settings. Roll each setting once.

**Target Thickness by Pasta Type:**

| Pasta Type | KitchenAid Setting |
|---|---|
| Pappardelle | 5 |
| Tagliatelle / Fettuccine | 5–6 |
| Lasagne sheets | 6–7 |
| Ravioli / Tortellini | 7–8 (hold sheet to light — you should see your hand through it) |

---

**Phase 4: The Boil**

Add water to your pot — no salt yet. Cover and bring to a full rolling boil. Once boiling, add the salt and stir to dissolve. Allow the water to return to a rolling boil before adding pasta.

> **To prevent boil-overs:** Wipe a thin film of olive oil around the inner rim of the pot, or rest a wooden spoon across the top.

Add pasta and cook per the chart below. Fresh pasta cooks fast — stay at the pot.

**Fresh Pasta Cook Times:**

| Pasta Type | Cook Time |
|---|---|
| Tagliatelle / Fettuccine | 1–2 min |
| Pappardelle | 2–3 min |
| Lasagne sheets (par-cook) | 2 min |
| Ravioli / Tortellini | 3–4 min |
| Gnocchi | Float + 30 sec |

> **Critical:** Do not drain through a colander. The flour sediment that settles at the bottom of the pot will coat your pasta in over-hydrated, sticky gluten paste. Use a spider skimmer or tongs to lift the pasta directly into your sauce.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

- **Flour Choice:** Both 00 and King Arthur Bread Flour work with this formula. 00 flour produces a silkier, more delicate sheet — the classic choice for rolled pasta. Bread flour has higher protein content, producing a slightly chewier, more rustic result. If using bread flour, extend the rest in Phase 2 to 45 minutes.
- **Egg vs. Water Pasta:** Egg yolks introduce fat, which shortens gluten strands slightly, producing a more tender, richer dough that rolls thinner with less resistance. Water pasta (orecchiette, cavatelli) relies entirely on gluten development for structure, producing a chewier result with better hold in heavy braises and ragù. Egg pasta's richness makes it the natural pairing for butter- and cream-based sauces.
- **Drying Before Cutting:** Allow rolled sheets to dry on a floured surface or pasta rack for 5–10 minutes before cutting. A slightly tacky but not wet sheet cuts cleanly without sticking to itself.
- **Make-Ahead:** Finished dough can be refrigerated wrapped in plastic for up to 24 hours. Return to room temperature for 20 minutes before rolling.
- **Variant:** For the rich yolk laminated version of this dough, see Recipe 12-07. For the spinach variant, see Recipe 12-10.

## Glossary

- **Gluten:** An elastic protein network formed when glutenin and gliadin in flour combine with water and are mechanically worked. Gives pasta its structure and chew.
- **Lamination (Pasta):** The process of folding and re-rolling pasta dough through the thickest roller setting to build structure and achieve a uniform rectangular sheet.
- **Well Method:** A hand-mixing technique where flour is mounded and hollowed at the center to contain wet ingredients, incorporated gradually from the inside out.
- **Spider Skimmer:** A wide, shallow wire-mesh tool used to lift pasta from cooking liquid without a colander.
- **Ball-Roll-Turn:** A hand-kneading method where the dough is pushed forward with the heel of the hand, rolled back into a ball, and rotated 90° before the next push — building even gluten development in all directions.
- **00 Flour:** Finely milled Italian flour with lower protein content (typically 9–11%). Produces a silky, extensible dough ideal for rolled pasta.
