# Café Athena - Lemon Blackberry Tart

A composed individual tart of ring-molded sweet shortcrust, filled with Foundation Lemon Curd and finished with fresh blackberries — classical French pâtisserie executed at the plated dessert register.

**Yield:** 6 individual tarts (90 mm rings) | **Prep:** 20 min (assembly only — shells and curd made separately) | **Cook:** 15 min (blind bake) | **Inactive:** 2 hr (curd sets in shells) | **Total:** ~2 hr 40 min

---

## Headnote

This tart is the assembly proof — the point where two foundational building blocks from Les Fonds become something that belongs on a restaurant pass. The shell is ring-molded from 12-02 Foundation Tart Dough (Sweet Shortcrust variant), blind-baked using the freezer method, then filled while warm with 12-24 Foundation Curd and finished with fresh blackberries arranged directly on the set curd. The interplay is deliberate: the butter-rich shortcrust provides a neutral, sandy counterpoint to the sharp acidity of the lemon curd, and the blackberries add color contrast, fresh aromatics, and a second acidic note that extends rather than duplicates the curd's brightness. **Teaching Idea: Blind Baking Cold Dough.** Standard blind baking uses weights to hold the dough against the walls of the pan and prevent puffing. The freezer method skips the weights by firming the fat before the shell goes into the oven — the butter hardens enough to hold the shell's shape through the early minutes of baking before the gluten and protein structure sets. This only works if the dough is genuinely cold; a shell that goes into the oven at room temperature will slump, regardless of how well it was shaped.

---

## Ingredients

**The Shells**

- 1 batch 12-02 Foundation Tart Dough, Sweet Shortcrust variant (approximately 200 g dough for 6 × 90 mm rings) — see 12-02 for the full dough recipe and rolling instructions

**The Curd**

- 1 batch 12-24 Foundation Curd (Lemon Protocol), approximately 370 g — see 12-24 for the full curd recipe; prepare this first so it is ready to pour when the shells come out of the oven

**For Finishing**

- 250 g (approximately 2 cups) fresh blackberries, rinsed and gently dried on paper towels
- powdered sugar, for dusting (optional)
- zest of 1 lemon (optional, added at plating for aromatic lift)

---

## Mise en Place

- Prepare 1 batch 12-02 Foundation Tart Dough (Sweet Shortcrust variant) per that folio's instructions; chill the finished dough for a minimum of 1 hour before rolling.
- Prepare 1 batch 12-24 Foundation Curd per that folio's instructions; keep warm in its saucepan or bain-marie at approximately 140°F/60°C, or reheat gently just before pouring.
- Set out six 90 mm stainless steel tart rings on a parchment-lined, flat baking sheet.
- Place a second sheet of parchment and a second baking sheet at the ready for weighting (if not using the freezer method).
- Preheat oven to 320°F/160°C.
- Rinse and gently dry the blackberries; spread on paper towels in a single layer.

---

## Method

**Phase 1: Roll and Line the Rings**

On a lightly floured work surface, roll the chilled dough to approximately 3 mm thickness — thin enough to be light and crisp when baked, thick enough to hold its shape off the ring. Cut strips approximately 30 mm wide (the interior height of the ring) and cut circles slightly larger than the interior diameter of the rings.

To line each ring: press a strip along the interior wall first, pressing it flush against the metal with your thumb and smoothing any overlap at the seam. Then press the base circle in, pressing it firmly into the angle between the wall and the base to seal. Run your thumb around the interior seam to ensure no gap exists — any gap will allow the curd to leak through during filling. Trim any overhang above the ring flush with a paring knife or bench scraper.

**Why:** Building sides first, then base, is the standard ring-mold approach for flat-bottomed tarts. It gives you better control over the wall thickness and prevents the base from being pushed up when you press the strip in.

**Phase 2: Chill and Set Before Baking**

Transfer the lined rings to the prepared baking sheet and place in the freezer for a minimum of 20 minutes. The dough should feel firmly set — not pliable — when you press it lightly with a fingertip. A shell that flexes is not cold enough; return it for another 10 minutes. The objective is to have the fat fully hardened before the shell enters the oven.

**Phase 3: Blind Bake**

Transfer the baking sheet directly from the freezer to the preheated 320°F/160°C oven. Bake for 14–16 minutes, until the shells are set, dry to the touch, and just beginning to take a light golden color at the edges and base. The walls should hold their shape without bulging inward. If any wall begins to collapse during baking, remove the tray, press the wall back flush against the ring with a clean folded cloth, and return to the oven immediately.

Do not overbake — a deeply golden shell will be too fragile to survive filling and will shatter when cut. You want a pale gold: fully cooked through, no raw-dough sheen at the base, but not toasted.

Allow the shells to cool in their rings on the baking sheet for 5 minutes. They will firm further as the butter resolidifies. Do not attempt to remove the rings at this stage.

**Phase 4: Fill with Curd**

Ensure the curd is warm — at or above 140°F/60°C — and fluid. If it has cooled and set, warm it gently in a bain-marie or in 10-second microwave bursts, stirring between each, until it flows freely from the spoon.

Pour the warm curd into each shell, filling to approximately 3 mm below the rim. Tap the baking sheet firmly on the counter two or three times to release any air bubbles and allow the surface to self-level. The curd should be smooth and glossy, with no visible craters or bubbles.

Slide the filled tarts — still in their rings, still on the baking sheet — into the refrigerator. Refrigerate for a minimum of 2 hours until the curd is fully set and holds a clean edge when pressed lightly at the center.

**Phase 5: Unmold and Finish**

To unmold: set each ring on a raised surface (a can or a jar works well) and press down firmly on the ring itself — the shell will release and drop cleanly onto your hand or a board. If the ring does not release easily, run a thin knife or offset spatula briefly around the exterior seam between the shell and the ring; do not force it.

Arrange the fresh blackberries on top of each tart, pressing each berry gently into the set curd so it is anchored — a blackberry that sits only on the surface will roll off when the plate is moved. Arrange from the center outward, or cluster them tightly toward one side of the tart for an asymmetric plated-dessert composition.

If using lemon zest: add a few strands directly to the berries at service — not before, as the oils will begin to desiccate on the cold curd surface within 30 minutes. Dust with powdered sugar immediately before the plate leaves the pass.

## Chef's Notes

- **Timing strategy:** The curd can be made up to 5 days ahead and stored refrigerated. The shells can be blind-baked up to 2 days ahead and held in an airtight container at room temperature. On service day, warm the curd, fill, set, and finish. Do not fill the shells more than 24 hours ahead — the shell will begin to soften.
- **Ring size alternatives:** This recipe scales cleanly to 75 mm rings (smaller, 8 tarts) or 110 mm rings (4 larger tarts). Adjust baking time by ±2 minutes for smaller/larger rings. Curd yield from one batch of 12-24 covers either format.
- **The freezer vs. weight method:** If the freezer is unavailable or the kitchen is very hot (above 78°F/25°C ambient), line the chilled shells with parchment and fill with baking weights or dried beans. Remove the weights and parchment after 10 minutes and return to the oven for the remaining 4–6 minutes to dry the base. The result is equivalent but slower.
- **Berry alternatives:** Raspberries are the most direct substitute — same acidity register. Blueberries read sweeter and less tart; add a few drops of lemon juice to the berries before arranging. Strawberries should be halved and patted dry — they release more liquid than other berries and can soften the curd surface if not dried thoroughly.
- **Sourcing the components:** 12-02 Foundation Tart Dough (Sweet Shortcrust variant) | 12-24 Foundation Curd (Lemon Protocol).

## Glossary

- Blind bake: To bake a pastry shell without a filling, either empty or weighted, in order to set the structure before a filling is added. Used when the filling does not require oven heat, or when it would be damaged by the time required to fully bake the shell.
- Ring mold: A stainless steel or aluminum ring without a base, used to shape individual tarts and pastries. The ring provides the wall structure; the shell must be strong enough to support itself once the ring is removed.
- Bain-marie: A gentle indirect heating method in which a container of food is set into or over a larger pan of hot water, preventing direct contact with a heat source. Used here to hold or rewarm curd without risking overheating.
- Nappé: The consistency at which a sauce or curd coats the back of a spoon in a continuous, even layer, and a line drawn through it with a fingertip holds clean. The curd is poured at this consistency or warmer.
- Pâte Sucrée: French term for a sweetened shortcrust pastry — literally "sugar dough." Richer and more cookie-like in texture than pâte brisée; the standard base for filled dessert tarts.
- Bench scraper: A flat metal or plastic blade used to portion dough, clean a work surface, and trim pastry flush with a ring or pan edge.
