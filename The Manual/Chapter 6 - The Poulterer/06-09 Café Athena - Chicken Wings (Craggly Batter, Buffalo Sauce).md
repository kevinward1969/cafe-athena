# Café Athena - Chicken Wings (Craggly Batter, Buffalo Sauce)

Shaggy, crag-built wings with a shattering crust — pre-rendered for dry skin, battered with intentional clumps, and finished in a classic butter-hot sauce emulsion.

**Yield:** 4 servings | **Prep:** 25 min | **Cook:** 45 min | **Total:** 1 hr 10 min

---

## Headnote

These are not smooth-coated wings. The crust is deliberately uneven — built from a batter system that seeds hydrated flour clumps into itself before you ever touch a wing. When those clumps hit the fryer, they bloom into shaggy, irregular spurs that shatter differently from clean-coated wings: some spots thick and crunchy, others thin and crackled. A low-oven pre-render step pulls subcutaneous fat from the skin before the batter goes on, so the fry is purely about crust development rather than also trying to cook the wing through. The Buffalo finish is canonical: a butter-forward emulsion built on Frank's RedHot, whisked off heat to keep it glossy, poured hot over freshly fried wings and served immediately.

**Teaching Idea: The Clump Batter.** Drizzling a portion of wet batter back into the dry mix before coating creates aggregates of hydrated flour suspended throughout the coating. These clumps fry at a different rate than the surrounding thin batter layer — they hold more moisture, take longer to set, and bloom outward as steam vents through them. The result is texture heterogeneity: no two bites are the same thickness or crunch. This cannot be replicated by double-dipping or adding more starch.

---

## Ingredients

### The Wings

- 1.4 kg (3 lb) whole chicken wings, broken into drumettes and flats (tips removed)
- 9 g (1½ tsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt
- 1.9 L (½ gal) neutral frying oil — peanut, canola, or rice bran
- Diamond Crystal kosher salt, for finishing — a pinch per batch, applied immediately out of the fryer

### The Dry Mix

- 350 g (2¾ cups) all-purpose flour
- 350 g (2¾ cups) potato starch
- 15 g (2½ tsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt
- 10 g (2 tsp) baking powder
- 8 g (1 tbsp) garlic powder
- 8 g (1 tbsp) onion powder

### The Wet Batter

- 240 g (1 cup) cold water — used to hydrate one-third of the dry mix into a loose batter before the remainder becomes the craggly coating

### The Buffalo Sauce

- 120 ml (½ cup) Frank's RedHot Original
- 57 g (4 tbsp) unsalted butter, cold, cut into tablespoon pieces
- 1 g (¼ tsp) garlic powder
- 5 ml (1 tsp) apple cider vinegar
- Diamond Crystal kosher salt, to taste

---

## Mise en Place

- Break down the wings: using a sharp knife or heavy shears, cut each whole wing at the two joints — separating drumette, flat, and tip. Reserve tips for stock or discard. Pat the drumettes and flats completely dry with paper towels.
- Season the wings with 9 g Diamond Crystal kosher salt, toss to coat, and place on a wire rack set inside a rimmed sheet pan. Do not stack.
- Preheat the oven to 250°F/120°C.
- Set out two medium bowls for the batter system (Bowl A, Bowl B).
- Cut the cold butter into tablespoon pieces and refrigerate until needed.

---

## Method

### Phase 1 — Pre-Render

1. Transfer the salted wings on their rack to the 250°F/120°C oven. Render for 15–20 minutes. The wings should look matte and slightly deflated — the subcutaneous fat has begun to liquefy and the skin surface is dry. The wings will not colour at this temperature; that is correct.

   **Why:** Wings carry significant fat directly under the skin. Rendering it out at low heat before frying gives the batter a dry, tacky surface to grip rather than a fat-slicked one. It also brings the interior to a safe temperature before the fry begins, so the fryer is doing crust work only.

2. Remove the wings from the oven and allow to cool on the rack for 5 minutes before battering.

### Phase 2 — Build the Batter

1. In Bowl A, whisk together all dry mix ingredients — flour, potato starch, salt, baking powder, garlic powder, and onion powder — until fully combined.

2. Transfer one-third of the dry mix (approximately 240 g) to Bowl B. Add the cold water slowly, whisking as you go, until you have a loose batter the consistency of thin pancake batter — it should fall off a spoon in a continuous ribbon, not drip in lumps or pour like water.

3. Slowly drizzle one-third of the wet batter from Bowl B back into Bowl A. Using a fork or your fingertips, drag and toss gently — you want irregular clumps of hydrated flour to form throughout the dry mix, ranging from pea- to marble-sized. Stop when the mix is visibly craggy. This is the craggly coating.

   **Why:** The clumps are the technique. When a wing is dragged through this coating, irregular aggregates of wet flour cling alongside the dry powder. In the fryer, the thin batter cooks through first while the clumps retain moisture longer and bloom outward — creating the shaggy, layered crust that shatters unevenly on the bite.

### Phase 3 — Coat

1. In batches of 4–5, toss the pre-rendered wings in Bowl A (the craggly dry mix). Press gently to encourage the clumps to adhere — do not aggressively shake off the excess.

2. Lift each wing individually and submerge in Bowl B (the wet batter), turning once to coat. Let excess drip for 2 seconds, then transfer to a clean wire rack. Do not stack the battered wings.

3. Allow the coated wings to rest on the rack for 5–10 minutes before frying. The batter will begin to dry slightly at the surface — this reduces slippage and batter loss in the oil.

### Phase 4 — Fry

1. Heat the oil in a large Dutch oven or heavy-bottomed pot to 360°F/182°C before the first batch drops. The wings will pull the temperature down immediately — target 350°F/175°C during active frying. Use a thermometer; do not estimate.

2. Lower 10–12 wings into the oil carefully using tongs or a spider. Do not drop from height. Fry undisturbed for 4–5 minutes.

3. Turn each wing once, then continue frying for a further 5–6 minutes until the crust is a deep amber and the clumps have set firm and hard.

   **Sensory Cue:** The sizzle should be vigorous and sustained throughout. If it drops to a low murmur, the oil temperature has fallen too far — pull the batch to a rack and allow the oil to recover to 360°F/182°C before continuing.

4. Transfer to a clean wire rack — not paper towels. Paper traps steam and softens the crust within seconds. Season with a pinch of Diamond Crystal kosher salt immediately off the fryer.

5. Allow the oil to return to 360°F/182°C before frying the next batch.

### Phase 5 — Buffalo Sauce and Finish

1. While the final batch fries, combine Frank's RedHot and the apple cider vinegar in a small saucepan over medium heat. Bring just to a simmer — do not boil.

2. Remove from heat. Add the cold butter pieces two at a time, whisking constantly after each addition until fully emulsified before adding the next. The finished sauce should be glossy, deeply orange-red, and coat the whisk rather than pool thin at the bottom.

   **Why:** Buffalo sauce is a warm emulsion — butterfat dispersed in hot acid liquid. Cold butter added off heat disperses before the temperature can break the emulsion. Adding butter over live heat or all at once causes the fat to separate out into a greasy pool rather than a glossy, cohesive sauce.

3. Add the garlic powder and stir to combine.

4. Season with a pinch of Diamond Crystal kosher salt, taste, and adjust.

5. Toss the fried wings in the Buffalo sauce immediately before serving — no more than 30 seconds before plating. The hot crust begins softening the moment sauce makes contact. Serve at once.

## Chef's Notes

### Other Sauces to Toss In

The craggly batter works with any toss-style sauce. Brief directions only:

- **Honey garlic** — equal parts honey and soy sauce, minced garlic, a splash of rice vinegar, reduced to a loose glaze
- **Gochujang** — gochujang paste thinned with honey, sesame oil, and rice wine vinegar
- **Garlic Parmesan** — melted butter with roasted garlic paste and finely grated Parmigiano-Reggiano; finish under the broiler for 2 minutes after tossing
- **Lemon pepper (dry finish)** — skip the sauce entirely; toss hot wings in fresh lemon zest, cracked black pepper, and a pinch of salt immediately out of the fryer
- **Nashville Hot** — for a full heat-paste build, see **06-04 Nashville Hot Chicken (The Weissman Method)** — the fire paste method translates directly to wings

### Blue Cheese or Ranch

Classic Buffalo service calls for celery sticks and a cold dipping sauce on the side. Blue cheese (Gorgonzola or Maytag) is canonical; ranch is the common American substitute. Both work as an acidic counterpoint to the butter-and-heat richness of the wing.

### Potato Starch Substitutes

Cornstarch is the most available substitute — the crust result is similar, with slightly less shatter and a finer crunch. Rice flour produces a thinner, more delicate crust. Potato starch produces the most pronounced crackle, particularly in the clumped sections, and is the recommended choice.

### Make-Ahead

The dry mix can be assembled and stored airtight for up to 2 weeks. The pre-render (Phase 1) can be done up to 2 hours ahead — rest the rendered wings at room temperature and batter when ready to fry.

## Glossary

- **Craggly batter:** A frying batter deliberately seeded with hydrated flour clumps before use. The clumps create irregular surface texture that fries at a different rate than the surrounding thin batter layer, producing a shaggy, heterogeneous crust that shatters unevenly.
- **Pre-render:** A low-temperature oven step applied to a protein before coating or frying. In wings, it melts subcutaneous fat from the skin, producing a drier surface for better batter adhesion and ensuring the interior reaches a safe temperature before the fry begins.
- **Subcutaneous fat:** Fat stored directly under the skin of a poultry wing. It does not render fully at frying temperatures alone — a pre-render step at low heat is required to liquefy it before frying.
- **Emulsion (warm):** A stable dispersion of one liquid in another — in Buffalo sauce, butterfat droplets dispersed in the hot sauce liquid. Maintained by whisking cold butter into hot liquid off heat. Overheating or adding butter too quickly breaks the dispersion.
- **Spider:** A wide, shallow wire-mesh skimmer used to lower, turn, and retrieve items from hot oil without puncturing the crust or causing oil splash.
- **Nappé:** The consistency at which a sauce coats the back of a spoon and holds a clean line when a finger is drawn through it. The finished Buffalo sauce should reach this consistency.
