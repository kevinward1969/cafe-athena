# Café Athena - The Definitive Guide to Egg Pasta

A master foundation dough yielding silky, golden pasta with a tender bite and clean egg flavor — the cornerstone of the Italian kitchen.

**Yield:** ~4 portions | **Prep:** 20 min | **Inactive:** 30 min | **Total:** ~50 min (plus rolling)

## Headnote

This is not a recipe — it is a skill. Egg pasta dough is one of the few preparations in the kitchen where your hands are the only real measuring tool that matters. The formula is fixed; the feel is learned. Every batch teaches you something the last one didn't. **Teaching Idea: Gluten Development.** When flour and water combine and are worked mechanically, the proteins glutenin and gliadin bond to form gluten — an elastic network that gives pasta its structure and chew. The rest period after mixing is not optional; it is when that network relaxes enough to be rolled thin without springing back.

## Mise en Place

- Weigh your flour and set aside in a mound or bowl
- Crack 2 whole eggs and 3 yolks into a bowl or measuring cup and weigh them. Note that weight. Subtract it from 285g — that is how much water you add
- Measure your water accordingly and combine with the eggs. Whisk briefly to combine — the goal is to marry the two, not to aerate
- If mixing by hand: clear a large work surface and have a bench scraper ready
- If using a food processor or stand mixer: have your machine ready before you begin
- Set out plastic wrap for resting

## Ingredients

### The Dough

- 300g (2 ½ cups) 00 flour or King Arthur Bread Flour
- 2 large eggs
- 3 large egg yolks
- Water — weigh your eggs, subtract from 285g, add that amount of water
- 1 pinch Diamond Crystal kosher salt

### For the Boil

- 6 quarts (5.7 L) water
- 65g (3 ½ tbsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt

## Method

**Phase 1: Mixing.**

**Option A — By Hand (The Well Method)**

Form the flour into a mound on your work surface. Create a wide, deep well in the center — wide enough to hold all your wet ingredients without breaking. Pour the egg and water mixture into the well along with the salt. Using a fork, begin beating the wet ingredients in the center while slowly dragging flour in from the inner walls. Work from the inside out — do not collapse the walls too quickly or the liquid will escape. Once the mixture becomes too thick for a fork, use your hands and bench scraper to bring the dough together. Knead using the ball-roll-turn method for 8–10 minutes until smooth and cohesive.

*Sensory Cue:* The dough should feel like warm Play-Doh — smooth, supple, not sticky, not cracking at the edges.

**Option B — Food Processor**

Add the flour first and pulse once to aerate. With the machine running, pour the egg mixture in through the feed tube. Process until the dough just comes together into a shaggy mass. Scrape every bit from the bowl onto your work surface and knead by hand using the ball-roll-turn method for an additional 5 minutes.

**Option C — Stand Mixer**

Add the wet ingredients to the bowl first. You can whisk them briefly by hand before adding — the goal is to combine, not to add air. Add the flour and salt. Mix on medium-low with the dough hook for 8–10 minutes. Scrape all bits from the bowl and hook and work them into the dough. Finish by hand using the ball-roll-turn method for 2 minutes.

**If the dough fights you:** If at any point during hand-finishing the dough feels rubbery and resists working, wrap it in plastic and rest for 15 minutes. This allows the gluten to relax. Finish kneading after the rest.

**If the dough is sticky:** Add flour 1 tsp at a time, working it in fully between additions.

---

**Phase 2: Rest.**

Form the dough into a smooth ball. Wrap tightly in plastic wrap — no exposed surfaces. Rest at room temperature for 30 minutes.

*Why:* The rest relaxes the gluten network developed during kneading. Without it, the dough will spring back when rolled and fight you at every thickness setting.

---

**Phase 3: Rolling (KitchenAid Pasta Roller Attachment).**

Divide the rested dough into 4 equal portions. Keep all portions not being worked wrapped in plastic — they will dry out quickly on contact with air.

Working one portion at a time, flatten it by hand into a rough rectangle no wider than the pasta roller opening.

**Step 1 — Lamination Pass:** Set the roller to its thickest setting. Pass the dough through 2–3 times. Fold the dough — first a half fold, then as it becomes more pliable, fold in thirds like a letter. This is lamination: it builds structure and drives you toward a clean rectangle.

**Step 2 — Progressive Thinning:** Once you have a good rectangle, begin moving through the settings one number at a time. Do not skip settings. Roll each setting once.

**Target Thickness by Pasta Type:**

| Pasta Type | KitchenAid Setting |
|---|---|
| Pappardelle | 5 |
| Tagliatelle / Fettuccine | 5–6 |
| Lasagne sheets | 6–7 |
| Ravioli / Tortellini | 7–8 (hold sheet to light — you should see your hand through it) |

---

**Phase 4: The Boil.**

Add water to your pot — **no salt yet**. Cover and bring to a full rolling boil. Once boiling, add the salt and stir to dissolve. Allow the water to return to a rolling boil before adding pasta.

*To prevent boil-overs:* Wipe a thin film of olive oil around the inner rim of the pot, or rest a wooden spoon or long chopstick across the top.

Add your pasta and cook per the chart below. Fresh pasta cooks fast — stay at the pot.

**Fresh Pasta Cook Times:**

| Pasta Type | Cook Time |
|---|---|
| Tagliatelle / Fettuccine | 1–2 min |
| Pappardelle | 2–3 min |
| Lasagne sheets (par-cook) | 2 min |
| Ravioli / Tortellini | 3–4 min |
| Gnocchi | Float + 30 sec |

**Critical:** Do not drain through a colander. The flour sediment that settles at the bottom of the pot will coat your pasta in over-hydrated, sticky gluten paste. Use a spider skimmer or tongs to lift the pasta directly into your sauce.

## Chef's Notes / Variations

- **Flour Choice:** Both 00 and King Arthur Bread Flour work with this formula. 00 flour produces a silkier, more delicate sheet — the classic choice for rolled pasta. Bread flour has higher protein content, producing a slightly chewier, more rustic result. If using bread flour, extend your room temperature rest in Phase 2 to 45 minutes to give the stronger gluten network more time to relax.
- **Egg vs. Water Pasta:** The primary difference between egg pasta and water-only pasta is fat and protein content. Egg yolks introduce fat, which shortens the gluten strands slightly, producing a more tender, richer dough that rolls thinner with less resistance. Water pasta (common in Southern Italian traditions — orecchiette, cavatelli) relies entirely on gluten development for structure, producing a chewier, more rustic result with better hold in heavy braises and ragù. Egg pasta's richness makes it the natural pairing for butter- and cream-based sauces.
- **Drying Before Cutting:** Once rolled, allow sheets to dry on a floured surface or pasta rack for 5–10 minutes before cutting. A slightly tacky but not wet sheet cuts cleanly without sticking to itself.
- **Make-Ahead:** Finished dough can be refrigerated wrapped in plastic for up to 24 hours. Return to room temperature for 20 minutes before rolling.

## Glossary

- **Gluten:** An elastic protein network formed when glutenin and gliadin in flour combine with water and are mechanically worked. Gluten gives pasta its structure and chew.
- **Lamination (Pasta):** The process of folding and re-rolling pasta dough through the thickest setting to build structure and achieve a uniform rectangular sheet.
- **Well Method:** A hand-mixing technique where flour is mounded and hollowed at the center to contain wet ingredients, which are gradually incorporated from the inside out.
- **Spider Skimmer:** A wide, shallow wire-mesh tool used to lift pasta, blanched vegetables, or fried items from liquid without a colander.
- **Ball-Roll-Turn:** A hand-kneading method where the dough is pushed forward with the heel of the hand, rolled back into a ball, and rotated 90° before the next push — building even gluten development in all directions.
- **00 Flour:** Finely milled Italian flour with lower protein content (typically 9–11%). The fine grind produces a silky, extensible dough ideal for rolled pasta.
