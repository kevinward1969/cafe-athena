# Café Athena - Silky Chicken Liver Mousse (Stovetop Method)

## A classic bistro spread achieved through careful pan-roasting and emulsification, requiring no specialized machinery.

Yield: 4–6 Ramekins (500 ml) | Prep: 40 min | Cook: 15 min | Set: 4–12 hr

**Headnote** The difference between a gritty pâté and a silky mousse is the coagulation of protein. In this stovetop method, we sauté the livers quickly to develop flavor (Maillard reaction) but remove them before the proteins fully tighten. We then create a stable fat-in-water emulsion by blending the warm protein with butter. **Teaching Idea: Carryover Cooking.** Meat continues to cook after it leaves the pan as heat equalizes from the surface to the center. To achieve a smooth texture, you must remove the livers when they are still quite pink inside (approx 140°F/60°C); the residual heat will finish the job safely as they rest.

**Mise En Place (Action Checklist)**

* **Clean the Livers:** Inspect livers individually. Use kitchen shears to trim away any white connective tissue (sinew) or green bile stains (which are incredibly bitter).
* **Temper the Butter:** Cut butter into cubes and leave at room temperature. It needs to be soft, not melted.
* **Bloom the Gelatin:** Sprinkle gelatin over cold water (for the gelée) and let it hydrate for 5 minutes.
* **Prepare the Blender:** Have your blender set up and ready; the emulsion must happen while the livers are warm.

**Ingredients**

**The Aromatics**

* 50 g (¼ cup) Shallots, minced
* 1 Clove Garlic, sliced thinly
* 30 g (2 tbsp) Unsalted Butter (for sautéing)
* 60 ml (¼ cup) Cognac or Brandy
* 2 g (2 sprigs) Fresh Thyme, leaves only

**The Forcemeat**

* 450 g (1 lb) Chicken Livers, cleaned and trimmed
* 225 g (1 cup) Unsalted Butter, room temperature (cubed)
* 60 ml (¼ cup) Heavy Cream
* 8 g (1 ½ tsp) Diamond Crystal Kosher Salt
* 2 g (½ tsp) Pink Curing Salt #1 (Optional, preserves pink color)
* 1 g (¼ tsp) Black Pepper, freshly ground

**The Port Gelée**

* 120 ml (½ cup) Ruby Port Wine
* 30 g (2 tbsp) Sugar
* 3 g (1 tsp) Powdered Gelatin (bloomed in 1 tbsp water)

**Method**

**Phase 1: The Sauté** In a large skillet, melt the **30 g butter** over medium-high heat until foaming. Add the **cleaned livers** in a single layer. Do not overcrowd the pan (sear in batches if necessary). Sear for 2 minutes on the first side until browned. Flip and sear for 1 minute on the second side. **Sensory Cue:** The livers should feel firm on the outside but squishy (like a rare steak) in the center. Add the **shallots**, **garlic**, and **thyme**. Cook for 1 minute until fragrant.

**Phase 2: The Deglaze** Pour in the **Cognac**. CAUTION: The alcohol may flame if using a gas burner. Simmer rapidly until the liquid has reduced by half and the livers are just cooked through but still pink in the center.

**Why:** Reducing the liquid concentrates the flavor and burns off the harsh alcohol notes. Remove from heat immediately. Let the pan sit for 2 minutes to allow **carryover cooking** to equalize the temperature.

**Phase 3: The Emulsion** Transfer the warm liver mixture (and all pan juices) into a blender. Add the **heavy cream**, **salt**, **pepper**, and **curing salt** (if using). Blend on high until completely liquified and smooth. With the blender running on low, drop in the **room temperature butter** one cube at a time. **Why:** Adding fat slowly creates a stable emulsion, preventing the mixture from breaking (separating into oil and solid).

**Phase 4: Refining and Casting** Pass the warm mixture through a fine-mesh sieve (chinois) into a clean bowl. **Why:** This removes any missed sinew or thyme stems, guaranteeing a Michelin-standard texture. Pour the mixture into ramekins, leaving ¼ inch of space at the top. Tap ramekins on the counter to release air bubbles. Refrigerate uncovered for **2 hours** until the surface is firm to the touch.

**Phase 5: The Port Gelée** In a small saucepan, warm the **Port wine** and **sugar** until the sugar dissolves. Do not boil. Stir in the **bloomed gelatin** until fully melted. Let the liquid cool to room temperature (lukewarm). **Critical:** If the jelly is too hot, it will melt the fat in the mousse and create a muddy layer. Gently pour a thin layer over the chilled mousse. Return to the fridge for **2 hours** to fully set.

**Chef's Notes / Variations**

* **The "Pink" Factor:** Home cooks are often scared of pink chicken. In whole muscles, this is a risk. In liver mousse, if you cook it until gray, the texture will be grainy and metallic. The target internal temp is 150°F (65°C).
* **Curing Salt:** We include Pink Salt #1 (Nitrite) for aesthetics (color retention) and flavor (cured taste), but it also acts as a safety hurdle against bacteria. It is optional but recommended.
* **Serving:** Allow the mousse to stand at room temperature for 15 minutes before serving to soften the butter for better spreadability.

**Glossary**

* **Carryover Cooking:** The phenomenon where food retains heat and continues to rise in temperature after being removed from the heat source.
* **Maillard Reaction:** The chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that gives browned food its distinctive flavor.
* **Deglaze:** To add liquid to a hot pan to dissolve the caramelized bits of food (fond) stuck to the bottom.
