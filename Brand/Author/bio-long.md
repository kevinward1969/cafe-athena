# Author Bio — Long (About page / press)

**Status:** Draft — Phase 2
**Last Updated:** 2026-06-11

---

Kevin Ward is not a chef. He is quick to say so, and he means it — not as a disclaimer, but as a positioning statement. The distinction between a chef and a serious home cook matters to him: one cooks under pressure for strangers; the other cooks with intention for people they love. He has been doing the second thing for most of his life.

His culinary education didn't happen in a kitchen classroom. It happened at family tables and in friends' homes, in books by Escoffier and Keller and Pépin, in hours of YouTube from Jean-Pierre and Marco Pierre White, in a personal cookbook library that long ago outgrew whatever shelf it was supposed to live on. He has never staged, never attended culinary school, never cooked professionally. What he has done is read obsessively, cook regularly, and pay close attention.

Café Athena grew out of a practical frustration. Ward had accumulated years of recipes across handwritten notes, digital files, and a sprawling OneNote archive — and none of it was accessible in the kitchen. A Samsung Smart Fridge with a large display screen made the obvious solutions incompatible. The workaround he built to solve that problem eventually became this: a full cookbook system, with a custom Astro website, a manuscript organized around professional kitchen structure, and an AI-assisted pipeline that helped him build and maintain it.

The name belongs to his wife. For years, Kevin had been posting casual cooking photos on Facebook and Instagram under the caption "Tonight at Café Athena." The name was already the project — he just eventually made it official.

The cookbook is organized the way a professional kitchen operates: technique first, then stations, then building blocks. The premise is that a home cook who understands the principles behind food doesn't need to be told what to do every time. Café Athena is Ward's attempt to write the book he wished had existed when he started cooking seriously — and to make it useful every time he stands at the counter.
