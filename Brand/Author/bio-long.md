# Author Bio — Long (About page / press)

**Status:** Draft — Phase 3
**Last Updated:** 2026-06-24

---

Kevin is not a chef. He'll say so before you ask — not to manage expectations, but because the distinction actually matters. A chef cooks under pressure, for strangers, on someone else's terms. Kevin cooks for people he loves, in a kitchen he knows, because he wants to. He has been doing this his entire adult life.

His culinary education happened at family tables, in friends' kitchens, and in books — Escoffier, Keller, Pépin — supplemented by years of footage from Jean-Pierre and Marco Pierre White. He has never staged, never cooked professionally. What he has is a serious reading habit, a lot of time at the stove, and a desire to understanding why something works rather than just knowing that it does.

That preference runs deep. His first recipe modification was his mother's apple pie, in high school, in the 1980s. The collection grew from there — a lifetime of recipes on index cards and scraps of paper, then a sprawling OneNote archive he kept on an iPad in the kitchen. When he and his wife got a Samsung Smart Fridge, the iPad setup stopped working with it. The screen was large and always on, but the OneNote interface was wrong for the context. Around the same time, he had started building an AI Chef Agent in Gemini to assist with recipe development and technique. These things came together. He built a site: something web-friendly, mobile-friendly, and fridge-screen-friendly, built to hold the archive he had been accumulating his whole life. The project exists because he loves cooking more than the considerable amount of time he has put into building it.

The name came from Facebook. For years he had been posting dinner photos with a line that read "Tonight at Café Athena." The name already felt like the project. He eventually made it official. Café Athena is because of his wife Athena — the name belongs to the same logic as the cooking itself: she is the reason the kitchen mattered, and the name says so without explaining it.

The cookbook is organized the way a professional kitchen operates: technique first in the Academy chapters, then stations across the Brigade, then the building-block preparations of the Larder. A cook who reads through The Academy understands why things happen at the stove. A cook who works through The Larder has a refrigerator full of preparations that make every Brigade recipe compound. The book is designed so that nothing you learn here stops with the recipe where you learned it.
